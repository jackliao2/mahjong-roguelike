Riichi Mahjong Rules: The Complete Beginner's Guide (2026)

Riichi mahjong is the Japanese variant of mahjong played by four players with 136 tiles. This guide walks through every rule you need to play your first real game, from wall building and turn order to melds, riichi declaration, yaku, and chombo penalties.

Riichi mahjong looks intimidating from the outside. There are 136 tiles, unfamiliar Japanese terms, and a scoring system that can take months to fully internalize. But the underlying rules are surprisingly clean: four players, a wall of tiles, and a race to assemble four melds plus one pair before anyone else. This guide covers every rule you need to sit down and play a real game of riichi mahjong in 2026, written for absolute beginners who want the rules explained clearly, step by step.

What Is Riichi Mahjong?

Riichi mahjong is the Japanese variant of mahjong, descended from Chinese mahjong but refined over decades into a tighter, more strategic form. It is played by exactly four players using a set of 136 tiles. The defining feature that gives the variant its name is the riichi declaration, a special bet a player can place when their hand is one tile away from winning. That single rule adds an entire layer of psychological pressure that does not exist in most other mahjong variants.

Unlike casual Chinese mahjong, riichi mahjong is played for points rather than for individual hand wins. Each player starts with 25,000 or 30,000 points, and the goal of a full game is to finish with more points than you started with, not just to win the most hands. A single hand can swing thousands of points, so every decision matters. For a deeper walkthrough of how a single hand plays out, read our step-by-step tutorial after you finish this rules guide.

Riichi mahjong is the most common variant played in clubs, online platforms, and tournaments worldwide. The rules described here follow the official ruleset used by the European Mahjong Association and the Japanese professional leagues, with notes where casual tables differ.

The Equipment: 136 Tiles, No Flowers, No Jokers

A riichi mahjong set contains exactly 136 tiles. There are 34 unique tile designs, and the set includes four identical copies of each. There are no flower tiles, no season tiles, and no jokers. Every tile in the wall is functional and can be part of a winning hand.

The Three Numbered Suits

The first 27 unique tiles are organized into three suits of nine tiles each. Each suit runs from 1 to 9, with four copies of each number.

  • Characters (manzu): The numbers 1 through 9, written as Chinese numerals over the character for "ten thousand."
  • Circles (pinzu): The numbers 1 through 9, drawn as circular coin patterns.
  • Bamboo (souzu): The numbers 1 through 9, drawn as sticks of bamboo. The 1 of bamboo is a special tile showing a single bird.

Within a suit, three consecutive numbered tiles (such as 2-3-4 of bamboo) form a sequence. Three identical tiles (such as three 5s of circles) form a triplet. Four identical tiles form a quad.

Honor Tiles: Winds and Dragons

The remaining seven unique tiles are honor tiles. There are four winds and three dragons, again with four copies of each.

  • Winds: East, South, West, North.
  • Dragons: White dragon (a blank tile, sometimes with a border), green dragon (the character for "green"), and red dragon (the character for "center," 中).

Honor tiles cannot form sequences. They only count as triplets, quads, or pairs. They are also where many of the easiest yaku come from, since a triplet of any dragon or your own seat wind is worth a yaku on its own.

Setting Up the Game: Wall, Deal, and Starting Player

At the start of a game, all 136 tiles are shuffled face-down on the table. Players then build the wall: each player assembles 17 stacks of two tiles in front of themselves, and the four walls are pushed together to form a hollow square in the middle of the table.

Once the wall is built, the dice are rolled to determine the dealer (called Oya, the East player) for the first hand. The dealer's seat determines the seat winds for that hand: the dealer is East, the player to the dealer's right is South, the player across is West, and the player to the dealer's left is North. These seat assignments rotate as the dealer position rotates.

The wall is then "broken" at a position determined by the dice roll. Tiles are dealt starting from that break point. Each player receives 13 tiles, dealt four at a time over three rounds, with a final single tile dealt to each player to reach 13. The dealer receives 14 tiles and takes the first turn. Every other player holds 13 tiles until their turn comes.

Five tiles from the wall are reserved as the dead wall (also called the wanpai). These cannot be drawn during normal play. They are used only for kan replacement draws and to reveal the dora indicators and ura-dora indicators that determine bonus tiles during the hand.

Turn Structure: Draw, Discard, and Claim

On your turn, you draw one tile from the live wall, bringing your hand to 14 tiles. You then choose one tile to discard, returning your hand to 13 tiles. That discarded tile is placed face-up in front of you in a row called the pond. Play then passes to the player on your right, who draws and discards in turn.

This draw-discard rhythm continues around the table until someone wins or the wall runs out. The order of play is always counter-clockwise in riichi mahjong: East plays first, then South, then West, then North, then back to East.

Claiming a Discard: Priority Order

Whenever a player discards a tile, the other three players have a brief moment to claim that tile before the next player draws. Claims have a strict priority order:

  1. Ron (win): If the discarded tile completes your hand and you have a yaku, you can call ron and win immediately. This has the highest priority and beats any other claim.
  2. Kan (quad): If you already hold three copies of the discarded tile, you can claim it to form a quad.
  3. Pon (triplet): If you already hold a pair of the discarded tile, you can claim it to form a triplet.
  4. Chi (sequence): Only the player whose turn is next can claim a discarded tile to form a sequence with two tiles already in hand.

If two players both want the same discard for different reasons, the higher-priority claim wins. Ron always beats a kan or pon claim. If two players can both ron on the same discard, the player closest in turn order to the discarder wins the claim, though some rulesets allow double ron or even triple ron.

If no one claims, the next player simply draws from the wall and play continues.

Meld Types: Chi, Pon, and Kan

A meld is a completed group of tiles within your hand. There are four types of melds in riichi mahjong: sequences, triplets, quads, and pairs. The first three can be formed either by drawing tiles yourself (called a closed meld) or by claiming a discard from another player (called an open meld).

Chi: The Sequence Call

A chi forms a sequence of three consecutive numbered tiles in the same suit, such as 4-5-6 of characters or 1-2-3 of bamboo. You can only call chi on a discard from the player whose turn comes immediately before yours. To call chi, you must already hold two of the three tiles in the sequence.

Sequences cannot wrap around the end of a suit. There is no 8-9-1 or 9-1-2 sequence. A 1-2-3 sequence is valid, as is a 7-8-9, but anything crossing the 9-1 boundary does not exist.

Calling chi opens your hand. Open hands cannot call riichi and lose access to certain closed-only yaku, so chi should be called with a clear plan for which yaku you are pursuing.

Pon: The Triplet Call

A pon forms a triplet of three identical tiles. Unlike chi, pon can be called on a discard from any opponent, not just the player to your left. You must already hold a pair of that tile to call pon.

Pon is the most flexible call because it can be made against any player. Triplet-forming calls are common when chasing honor-tile yaku, since a triplet of any dragon or your own seat wind immediately grants a yaku.

Kan: The Quad Call

A kan forms a quad of four identical tiles. There are two ways to declare kan:

  • Open kan (daiminkan): You already hold a triplet of a tile, and an opponent discards the fourth copy. You claim it to form the quad.
  • Closed kan (ankan): You already hold all four copies in your closed hand, and you declare the quad on your own turn without claiming a discard.
  • Added kan (shouminkan): You already have an open triplet from a previous pon call, and you later draw the fourth copy yourself. You can promote the triplet into a quad.

Whenever a kan is declared, two things happen. First, an additional tile called the rinshan tile is drawn from the dead wall to replace the extra tile consumed by the quad, so the player's hand size stays correct. Second, an additional dora indicator is flipped on the dead wall, which can dramatically increase the value of hands involving that suit.

How to Win: Four Melds and One Pair

A complete winning hand in riichi mahjong contains four melds plus one pair, for a total of 14 tiles. The four melds can each be a sequence, a triplet, or a quad. The pair is two identical tiles. There is one special exception: the seven pairs yaku, which is a hand of seven different pairs and bypasses the standard four-meld structure.

There are two ways to win a hand:

  • Tsumo: You draw the winning tile yourself from the wall. Payment comes from all three opponents.
  • Ron: An opponent discards the tile that completes your hand, and you claim it. Payment comes only from the player who discarded.

The Yaku Requirement

Completing four melds and a pair is not enough to win. Your hand must also contain at least one yaku, which is a recognized scoring pattern. A hand that is structurally complete but contains no yaku is called a no-ten or invalid hand, and you cannot declare a win on it.

Yaku are the heart of riichi mahjong strategy. They are why the same shape of tiles can be worth 1000 points in one hand and 12,000 in another. Common beginner yaku include riichi, tanyao, and pinfu, but there are more than 40 in total. For the full list with hand examples and values, visit our complete yaku list.

The Riichi Declaration: The Ready Hand Bet

The riichi declaration is the signature mechanic of riichi mahjong and the source of the variant's name. When your hand is one tile away from winning (a state called tenpai), you may declare riichi on your turn after drawing a tile. Doing so commits you to a specific winning tile.

Requirements for Riichi

  • Your hand must be closed (no chi, pon, or open kan calls). Closed kan (ankan) is allowed since it does not technically open the hand.
  • You must be in tenpai: one tile away from a winning hand with at least one yaku.
  • You must have at least 1000 points to bet, which you place on the table as a 1000-point stick.
  • You must declare "riichi" aloud and place your discard sideways to mark the turn.

After declaring riichi, you cannot change your hand. On every subsequent turn, you draw a tile, and if it is not your winning tile, you must discard it. The only modification allowed is declaring a closed kan that does not change your waiting shape.

Benefits of Riichi

The rewards for declaring riichi are significant:

  • You gain the riichi yaku (1 han), which counts toward your minimum yaku requirement.
  • If you win on the very next discard cycle, you gain ippatsu, a separate yaku worth 1 additional han.
  • You gain access to ura-dora: bonus tiles revealed under the regular dora indicators on the dead wall, which only riichi winners can use.
  • Your declaration pressures opponents, who often shift to defensive play to avoid dealing into your hand.

The trade-off is risk. If another player wins the hand, or the hand ends in a draw without you winning, you lose your 1000-point stick. If you declare riichi and another player wins by tsumo, you also pay the tsumo penalty as if you were tenpai (which you are). For help picking good moments to declare, see our section on waiting tiles — the quality of your wait should heavily influence your riichi decision.

Yaku Basics: How Hands Earn Value

A yaku is a recognized scoring pattern built into your winning hand. Some yaku are about tile composition (all triplets, all one suit), some are about hand state (closed hand, riichi declared), and some are about win conditions (won on a self-draw, won as the dealer). Every yaku is worth a number of han, the basic unit of hand value.

Three beginner-friendly yaku form the backbone of most early wins:

  • Riichi: Awarded for declaring riichi. Worth 1 han. The most common yaku in the entire game because it is purely a decision, not a tile composition.
  • Tanyao: A closed hand with no terminals (1s or 9s) and no honors. All melds and the pair use tiles 2 through 8 only. Worth 1 han.
  • Pinfu: A closed hand with four sequence melds and a pair that is not a dragon or your seat wind. The pair must also wait on a ryanmen (two-sided wait). Worth 1 han.

Stacking yaku is how high-value hands form. A closed hand that declares riichi, wins by tsumo, is all-sequences, and uses only 2-8 tiles could simultaneously earn riichi, tsumo, tanyao, and pinfu for 4 han before any dora. Add dora tiles and the score climbs quickly.

Beyond these basics, there are yaku for flushes (one suit only), triplets of dragons, all-triplet hands, seven pairs, and many more. Some are easy to plan from the deal; others require specific conditions. The full list with hand examples and han values is in our complete yaku list.

Scoring Basics: Han and Fu

Riichi mahjong scoring is detailed enough to deserve its own guide, so this section is brief. Two numbers define every hand's value: han and fu.

  • Han is the multiplier. Each yaku adds han, and each dora tile adds han. Han is the dominant factor in scoring.
  • Fu is the base value, calculated from the type of melds (triplets are worth more than sequences), the winning tile (closed ron is worth more than tsumo), and the pair (dragons and seat winds add fu).

Hands with 5 or more han are scored by han alone (a system called mangan and above), which simplifies things considerably. Below 5 han, both han and fu feed into a formula that produces the final point value. A typical beginner hand is worth around 1000 to 3000 points; a mangan is 8000 base points, which translates to 12,000 from a non-dealer ron or 4000 per player on a dealer tsumo.

You do not need to memorize the formula to play. Most players use a scoring chart or app for the first few months. Focus on building hands with valid yaku and let scoring come naturally.

Game Flow: East Round and South Round

A standard game of riichi mahjong is called a hanchan, or "half game." A hanchan consists of an East Round followed by a South Round. A shorter format called tonpuusen is East Round only.

Each round is divided into individual hands called kyoku. The first hand is East 1, then East 2, and so on. The dealer is always East for the current hand. If the dealer wins the hand, or the hand ends in a draw with the dealer in tenpai, the dealer stays East and an extra hand is played in the same kyoku. If the dealer loses or is not in tenpai on a draw, the dealer position rotates to the next player counter-clockwise, and the kyoku number advances.

East Round continues until every player has been dealer once (East 1 through East 4, with possible extra hands from dealer wins or draws). Then South Round begins, and the same cycle repeats from South 1 through South 4. After South 4 ends, the game is over and final scores are tallied. If the leader at the end of South 4 is below a target score (often 30,000 points), play continues into West Round as an extension.

This dealer rotation system is why winning as the dealer is so valuable. A dealer win not only pays more (a 1.5x multiplier called oya-kake), but it also grants an extra hand to keep stacking wins. A skilled player who strings together dealer wins can swing a game from last to first in a single rotation.

Draws and Abortive Draws: Ryuukyoku

If the wall is exhausted and no one has won, the hand ends in a draw called ryuukyoku. There are two main types of draws:

Exhaustive Draw

An exhaustive draw happens when the live wall is fully drawn down to the dead wall (typically 14 tiles of dead wall remain untouched) and no player has won. Players then reveal their hands and declare whether they were in tenpai. Players in tenpai split a small point bonus from players who were not in tenpai. The dealer rotates unless they were in tenpai, in which case they repeat.

Abortive Draws

Certain conditions end a hand early before the wall runs out, called abortive draws:

  • Four kans abort: If four quads are declared by different players, or a single player declares four quads and is not in tenpai, the hand aborts.
  • Four winds abort: If all four players discard the same wind tile on their first discard, the hand aborts.
  • Four riichi abort: If all four players declare riichi, the hand aborts (extremely rare).
  • Nagashi mangan: If the wall is exhausted and a single player has discarded only terminals and honors throughout the entire hand with no calls against those discards, that player may claim a special mangan-value payout instead of a normal tenpai split.

Abortive draws are uncommon in casual play but matter in tournament settings where a player wants to deny a high-value hand. Most casual games only see exhaustive draws.

Penalty Tiles: Chombo

A chombo is an illegal play that invalidates the hand and forces the offender to pay a penalty to all three opponents. Chombo penalties are severe: typically a mangan-value payment to each player, which can swing a game by 12,000 points or more.

Common chombo situations include:

  • Declaring a win on a hand with no yaku (false ron or false tsumo).
  • Declaring riichi on a hand that is not actually in tenpai.
  • Changing your hand after declaring riichi.
  • Calling ron on a tile that does not actually complete your hand.
  • Having too many or too few tiles in your hand (a fused hand or short hand).
  • Declaring a closed kan that changes your waiting shape after riichi.

In friendly play, minor mistakes like an accidental draw from the wrong end of the wall are usually handled with a small rewind or warning rather than a chombo. In tournament play, rules are stricter. If you are new to the game, expect to make at least one chombo mistake during your first few sessions — it is a rite of passage, not a sign you are bad at the game. Practical Tips for First-Time Players

The rules above are enough to play, but reading rules is not the same as playing well. Here are a few practical tips that help new players survive their first games without drowning in mistakes.

  • Memorize three yaku first. Riichi, tanyao, and pinfu will carry you through your first 50 hands. Do not try to learn all 40+ yaku at once. Learn the basics cold, then expand.
  • Keep your hand closed. Resist the urge to call chi or pon early on. Closed hands have access to more yaku and can declare riichi. Open hands lock you out of the most powerful patterns.
  • Declare riichi often. As a beginner, declaring riichi is almost always correct when you reach tenpai in a closed hand. It guarantees a yaku, pressures opponents, and gives you ura-dora upside.
  • Watch the discards. Your opponents' ponds tell you what they are not collecting. If a player has discarded three 5s of circles, no one can win on a 5 of circles sequence, and triplet waits on that tile are gone too.
  • Fold when behind. If a player declares riichi and your hand is far from tenpai, throw away safe tiles and pass the hand. Trying to out-race a riichi is one of the most common ways new players lose huge points.
  • Count the dora. Always know which tiles are dora for the current hand. A hand with three dora tiles is worth dramatically more than a hand with none, and that should change how aggressively you push.

Learn the Rules, Then Drill the Patterns

Reading the rules is step one. The real skill in riichi mahjong is recognizing shapes instantly: which discard takes you to tenpai, which wait gives you the most winning tiles, which yaku your hand is naturally chasing. The fastest way to build that pattern recognition is targeted practice, not just playing more games.

Why it matters: Players who drill hand-reading shapes outside of real games improve 3-5x faster than players who only learn by playing. Pattern recognition compounds quickly once you start training it deliberately.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many tiles are in riichi mahjong?

Riichi mahjong uses 136 tiles: 34 unique tile types with four copies of each. There are 27 numbered suit tiles (1 through 9 of characters, circles, and bamboo) plus seven honor tiles (four winds and three dragons). Riichi mahjong does not use flowers, seasons, or jokers.

What is riichi in mahjong?

Riichi is a special declaration a player makes when their hand is one tile away from winning (tenpai). The player places a 1000-point stick on the table as a bet and cannot change their hand afterward. In exchange, the hand gains the riichi yaku, opens access to ippatsu and ura-dora bonus tiles, and pressures opponents into defensive play.

How do you win at riichi mahjong?

To win, your hand must contain four melds plus one pair (14 tiles total) and include at least one yaku. A yaku is a qualifying pattern such as riichi, tanyao, or pinfu. You win either by drawing the final tile yourself (tsumo) or by claiming an opponent's discard that completes your hand (ron).

What is a yaku and why do I need one?

A yaku is a recognized scoring pattern in riichi mahjong, such as all triplets, a flush of one suit, or a closed hand with a riichi declaration. Every winning hand must contain at least one yaku, otherwise it is an invalid win. Yaku determine the base value of your hand and stack to increase the score. For the full list, see our complete yaku reference.

What is the difference between chi, pon, and kan?

Chi is a sequence call made from a discarded tile, and only the player to your left can do this. Pon is a triplet call made from any opponent's discard using a pair you already hold. Kan is a quad call that uses all four copies of a tile and triggers an extra tile draw called a rinshan tile. Kan also opens a new dora indicator.

How long does a game of riichi mahjong take?

A standard hanchan (East-South half game) lasts about 30 to 60 minutes depending on player speed. A tonpuusen (East-only game) is shorter, around 15 to 30 minutes. Online platforms often enforce time limits per turn, which keeps most games close to the lower end of these ranges.

Final Thoughts

Riichi mahjong rewards patience and pattern recognition more than luck. The rules described here are enough to sit down at any table, online or in person, and play a real game. Everything beyond this guide is refinement: better yaku recognition, smarter riichi timing, sharper defense, faster tile efficiency. None of that comes from reading. It comes from playing and drilling.

Start by mastering the three beginner yaku (riichi, tanyao, pinfu). Memorize the four melds and one pair structure. Practice reading waiting tiles until you can do it instantly. Once those fundamentals are second nature, the rest of the game opens up.
