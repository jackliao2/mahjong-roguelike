MAHJONG VARIANTS EXPLAINED

Riichi vs Chinese Mahjong: What's the Difference?

A complete guide to the two most popular mahjong variants in the world — covering rules, tiles, scoring, strategy, and which one you should learn first.

I played Chinese mahjong for years before touching riichi. The first time I saw someone declare riichi, locking their hand for a 1,000-point bet on a tile they couldn't change, I figured they were bluffing. They weren't. That single mechanic captures the biggest difference between Japanese riichi mahjong and Chinese mahjong. They share tiles and a 19th-century ancestor, but they play like completely different games. This guide untangles the actual differences so you can pick the right variant and start playing.

Both games descend from the same 19th-century Chinese ancestor. A century of independent evolution did the rest.

Riichi mahjong became a defensive, structured, high-stakes game with deep strategy. Chinese mahjong stayed faster and more flexible, a gambling-oriented game built for aggressive play and quick rounds. Pick the version that fits how you like to play.

Let's dig into each area, starting with the physical differences and working up to strategy and culture.

Tile Sets and Equipment

The most visible difference between Japanese and Chinese mahjong starts with the tiles. Both games use the same three numbered suits: Manzu (characters, 1–9), Pinzu (circles, 1–9), and Souzu (bamboo, 1–9). Add four winds (East, South, West, North) and three dragons (White, Green, Red) and you get 34 unique tile types, four copies each, 136 tiles total.

Chinese mahjong adds eight bonus tiles: four flowers (plum, orchid, chrysanthemum, bamboo) and four seasons (spring, summer, autumn, winter). These get drawn from the wall and set aside for bonus points; they don't participate in hand-building. That brings the Chinese set to 144 tiles.

Physical Differences Between Sets

Japanese riichi sets are typically smaller and lighter than Chinese sets. Riichi tiles measure roughly 26mm × 19mm × 16mm, while Chinese tiles are often 32mm × 24mm × 20mm or larger. This matters in practice. Riichi players manipulate the wall, draw tiles, and discard at speed, and smaller tiles are easier to handle.

Japanese sets also include four red five tiles (aka dora), one red replacement for each suit's normal 5, which count as bonus dora when included in a winning hand. These are optional but ubiquitous in modern riichi play. Chinese sets have no equivalent.

Key takeaway: If you own a Chinese set, you can play riichi by removing the flower and season tiles. If you own a Japanese set, you can play Chinese mahjong by playing without flowers (or using substitute markers). The core 136 tiles are identical across both variants.

Core Rules Differences

Both variants share the same fundamental goal: build a hand of 14 tiles consisting of four melds (triplets or sequences) and one pair. But the path to victory diverges sharply from there.

The Yaku Requirement: Riichi's Defining Feature

In Chinese mahjong, any complete hand of four melds + one pair is a valid winning hand. You can win with a hand of random tiles as long as the structure is correct. The game rewards speed and aggression. Whoever completes a hand first wins the round.

In riichi mahjong, a winning hand must also satisfy at least one yaku, a specific winning pattern or condition. Nothing else captures the gap between these two games as cleanly. You cannot win with a "chicken hand" (a structurally complete but patternless hand). If your hand has no yaku, you must either draw a tile that creates one or declare riichi (which is itself a yaku).

That requirement changes everything. In Chinese mahjong, you chase any hand aggressively. In riichi, you constantly evaluate: "Does my current hand direction lead to a valid yaku? If not, how do I pivot?"

The Furiten Rule: Defense Becomes Mandatory

Riichi mahjong has the furiten rule, which has no equivalent in Chinese mahjong. A player is in furiten (and cannot win by ron, calling another player's discard) if any of their own discarded tiles could complete their hand. In practice, this means:

  • If you've discarded a 4-man and your hand is waiting on a 4-man, you cannot win by ron. You must draw the tile yourself (tsumo).
  • If you pass on a winning tile (decline to call ron), you enter temporary furiten and cannot win by ron until your next draw.
  • If another player declares riichi, you can use their discards to figure out which tiles are safe. Furiten means they cannot win on tiles they've already discarded.

The furiten rule is the engine of riichi's defensive play. It makes reading opponents' discards essential and punishes reckless aggression. Chinese mahjong has no such restriction. You can win on any discard that completes your hand, regardless of your own discard history.

Melding Rules: Open vs Closed Hands

Both games let you call pon (triplet from a discard), chi (sequence from the player to your left's discard), and kan (quad). But in riichi, opening your hand reduces or eliminates many yaku. A hand worth 3 han closed might drop to 1 han when opened, or become completely invalid. Open your hand for speed, or keep it closed for value? That tension never goes away.

Chinese mahjong has no such penalty. Opening your hand is purely beneficial. It gets you to a winning hand faster, and the scoring system doesn't care how you got there.

Riichi's Dead Wall and Kan Mechanics

Riichi mahjong reserves the last 14 tiles of the wall as a dead wall (wangpai). These tiles are never drawn normally. They're used exclusively for:

  • Replacement draws after a kan declaration (you draw from the dead wall instead of the live wall)
  • Dora indicator: the third tile from the end of the dead wall is flipped to reveal the dora (the next tile in sequence counts as bonus han)
  • Ura-dora: revealed only after a riichi win, these are tiles under the dora indicator that add extra bonus han

Chinese mahjong has no dead wall. Kan calls draw from the end of the live wall, and the game simply ends when the wall is exhausted. That makes riichi's wall management an additional strategic layer that doesn't exist in Chinese play.

Scoring Systems

The scoring gap between riichi and Chinese mahjong is enormous, and it's where most new players get confused.

Riichi Scoring: Han × Fu

Riichi mahjong uses a two-part scoring system: han (multipliers from yaku and dora) and fu (mini-points from hand composition). The formula is roughly:

Base Points = Fu × 2^(2 + Han)

In practice, most players use scoring tables. The key insight: each additional han roughly doubles your score. A 1-han hand is worth ~1,000 points. A 4-han hand is worth ~8,000 points. A 13+ han hand (kazoe yakuman) is worth 32,000 points.

Riichi scoring also has mangan caps, specific point thresholds where the formula stops and a fixed payout applies:

Han Count Name Dealer Payout Non-Dealer Payout
1–4 Normal Formula-based Formula-based
5 Mangan 12,000 8,000
6–7 Haneman 18,000 12,000
8–10 Baiman 24,000 16,000
11–12 Sanbaiman 36,000 24,000
13+ Kazoe Yakuman 48,000 32,000

There are also yakuman hands, special pattern-based hands worth a fixed 32,000 points (48,000 for dealer) regardless of the han count. These include rare patterns like Thirteen Orphans (kokushi musou), Four Concealed Triplets (suu ankou), and Big Three Dragons (daisangen).

Chinese Mahjong Scoring: Fan × Points

Chinese mahjong scoring is simpler but varies enormously by region. The most common system (Hong Kong style) assigns a fixed point value per fan (winning pattern). A hand with 3 fan might be worth 3 × base points, while a hand with 8 fan might be worth 8 × base points. There is no fu component and no mangan cap. The score just keeps multiplying.

However, "Chinese mahjong" isn't one game. Different regions play different variants:

  • Hong Kong (Cantonese) style: 13 tiles, minimum 3 fan to win, flower tiles optional, fast-paced. The most popular Chinese variant in the West.
  • Chinese Official (Guobiao) Mahjong: Created in 1998 to standardize international competition. 81 fan patterns, minimum 8 points to win. Closer to riichi in complexity.
  • Sichuan (Bloody) Mahjong: Played without honor tiles (winds/dragons). Multiple players can win from the same discard. Extremely fast and aggressive.
  • Taiwanese Mahjong: 16-tile hands (5 melds + 1 pair), more complex than 13-tile variants.
  • Filipino Mahjong: 16 tiles, includes joker tiles, very different from all other variants.

When English speakers say "Chinese mahjong" without qualification, they usually mean Hong Kong style or the simplified version taught in Western mahjong classes. Always clarify which variant you're playing before sitting down.

Scoring Comparison: Same Hand, Different Outcomes

Take a simple hand: All Sequences (Pinfu), closed, with a Red Dragon pair. In both games, this is a modest hand. Here's how it scores:

  • Riichi mahjong: Pinfu (1 han) + closed tsumo (1 han) = 2 han, ~2,000 points. If you declared riichi (1 han) and had one dora (1 han), it jumps to 4 han, ~8,000 points. That's a fourfold increase from the riichi bet and luck alone.
  • Hong Kong Chinese mahjong: All Sequences (1 fan) + Self-Draw (1 fan) = 2 fan, typically 2 × base points. No further multipliers. The hand is what it is.

This is the core scoring philosophy difference. Riichi scoring is volatile; the riichi bet and dora tiles can dramatically inflate a hand's value. Chinese scoring is stable. You know roughly what your hand is worth from the start.

Yaku and Winning Patterns

Riichi mahjong has a structured, standardized yaku list recognized by every riichi player worldwide. There are roughly 40 yaku, organized by han value (1–6 han, plus yakuman). Every competitive riichi game uses the same list. A player from Tokyo, a player from São Paulo, and a player from Berlin can sit down and play without negotiating rules.

Common 1-han yaku include:

  • Riichi: Declaring a ready hand with a 1,000-point bet (closed hand only)
  • Tanyao — All simples (tiles 2–8 only, no terminals or honors)
  • Pinfu: All sequences, closed wait, valueless pair
  • Yakuhai: Triplet of any dragon, or triplet of the round/seat wind
  • Iipeikou — Two identical sequences (closed hand only)

For a full breakdown of every yaku, see our complete yaku list.

Chinese mahjong does not have a single standardized yaku list. Each regional variant has its own set of fan patterns, and even within the same variant, house rules vary. The Chinese Official (Guobiao) rulebook lists 81 fan patterns (the most comprehensive), while Hong Kong style might use 30–40. The lack of standardization is both a strength (flexibility) and a weakness (you must negotiate rules before every game).

The Riichi Declaration: Mahjong's Most Unique Mechanic

RIICHI EXCLUSIVE

What Is Riichi?

The riichi declaration is the defining mechanic of Japanese mahjong, and it has no equivalent in any other variant. Here's how it works:

  1. Your hand is closed (no called tiles from opponents) and tenpai (one tile away from winning).
  2. On your turn, you declare "riichi" by placing a 1,000-point stick on the table and turning your discard sideways.
  3. From that moment, you cannot change your hand. You must discard every tile you draw, unless that tile completes your hand (tsumo) or you can declare a concealed kan that doesn't change your wait pattern.
  4. If you win, you reveal the ura-dora (tiles under the dora indicator in the dead wall), which can add bonus han and dramatically increase your score.
  5. If you lose (someone else wins or the round ends in a draw), you must pay the 1,000-point stick to the winner or leave it on the table for the next winner.

The riichi declaration is a bet. You're betting 1,000 points that you'll win the hand, and you're telling everyone at the table that you're ready. The psychology shifts instantly. Other players know you're in tenpai and must decide whether to defend (discard safe tiles) or push (continue building their own hands).

Why it matters: Riichi creates a poker-like bluffing dynamic. You can declare riichi on a cheap hand to scare opponents into folding, or you can stay silent (dama) on a monster hand to trap someone into dealing in. This psychological layer is entirely absent from Chinese mahjong.

Picture this: you're in the last hand of a tournament, sitting in third place. You draw a tile that puts you one away from a win. You could play it safe, or you could declare riichi, locking your hand but doubling your potential score. That tension doesn't exist in Chinese mahjong. It's the reason riichi has produced more competitive drama in 20 years than Chinese mahjong has in a century.

The riichi bet is also why Japanese mahjong is called "riichi mahjong" in the first place. The mechanic is so central to the game's identity that it became the name.

Dora Tiles: Bonus Points Unique to Riichi

Dora (ドラ) are bonus tiles that add one han each to a winning hand. They are not yaku. They don't make a hand valid, but they inflate the score of a hand that already has at least one yaku. The dora system works like this:

  1. At the start of each hand, the dora indicator is flipped from the dead wall. This tile points to the dora: the next tile in sequence. For example, if the indicator is a 3-man, the dora is 4-man. If the indicator is a 9-pin, the dora wraps to 1-pin. Wind indicators point to the next wind (East→South→West→North→East).
  2. Every copy of the dora tile in your winning hand adds 1 han. If 4-man is the dora and you have three 4-man tiles in your hand, that's 3 bonus han.
  3. When a kan is declared, a new dora indicator is flipped, adding a new dora tile. This is called kan-dora.
  4. When a riichi declarer wins, the ura-dora (tiles under each dora indicator) are revealed, adding even more bonus han.
  5. Japanese sets also include aka dora (red fives): one red 5-man, one red 5-pin, and one red 5-sou, each worth 1 bonus han.

Dora introduces a push-your-luck element to riichi mahjong. A hand with 1 han from yaku and 3 han from dora is worth 4 han, the same as a hand with 4 han from pure yaku. This means you can win big with a "cheap" hand if you get lucky with dora, and it makes tile efficiency (holding onto potential dora tiles) a core skill.

Chinese mahjong has no dora equivalent. Flower and season tiles provide bonus points, but they're drawn individually (not revealed in advance) and don't create the same risk-reward dynamic.

Which One Should You Learn?

If you're reading this site, you should probably learn riichi. The yaku system makes it a better game, full stop. (Yes, I know some Chinese mahjong players will disagree. That's fine. Both games are great. But they scratch different itches.)

That said, the "right" answer still depends on your situation. Here's how I'd break it down.

Learn Chinese Mahjong If...

  • You want to play with family or at social gatherings. Chinese mahjong (especially Hong Kong style) is the default variant in most diaspora communities.
  • You prefer simpler rules and faster games. No yaku requirement means you can win with any complete hand.
  • You enjoy gambling. Chinese mahjong is traditionally played for money, and the scoring system is designed for cash settlement.
  • You have a Chinese tile set with flowers and seasons and want to use the full set.
  • You want to play right now with minimal study. A complete beginner can learn Chinese mahjong basics in 15 minutes.
OUR PICK

Learn Riichi Mahjong If...

  • You enjoy deep strategy and competitive play. Riichi's yaku system, furiten rule, and defensive play create a richer strategic landscape.
  • You want to play online. The two largest mahjong platforms, Mahjong Soul and Tenhou, are riichi-only. There is no Chinese mahjong platform with comparable player counts.
  • You discovered mahjong through anime, video games, or esports. The Yakuza series, Final Fantasy XIV, Akagi, Saki, and Mahjong Soul all feature riichi mahjong.
  • You want a standardized ruleset that works everywhere. Riichi rules are consistent worldwide. You never have to negotiate house rules.
  • You want to learn riichi mahjong through bite-sized puzzles. Mahjong Quiz turns riichi rules into 4-option multiple-choice questions with roguelike chapters and relics.
Our recommendation: If you're learning alone (online resources, video games, browser games), start with riichi. The standardization, online community, and wealth of learning materials make it the most accessible path for a solo learner. If you're learning with a group of friends who already play Chinese mahjong, learn their variant. The social experience is worth the rules ambiguity.

How Riichi Mahjong Became Popular in the West

For most of the 20th century, "mahjong" in the West meant Chinese mahjong, specifically the simplified American version popularized in the 1920s by Joseph P. Babcock, which added joker tiles and special hands. Riichi mahjong was virtually unknown outside Japan until the 2000s.

That changed dramatically over the last two decades. Here's what drove the shift:

Anime and Manga

The anime Akagi (2005) introduced millions of Western viewers to riichi mahjong. It's a psychological thriller about a genius mahjong gambler, and its over-the-top depiction of high-stakes matches, complete with dramatic internal monologues about tile efficiency and wait reading, made mahjong look like the most intense game on Earth. Saki (2009) followed with a sports-anime approach, featuring a high school mahjong club where characters had supernatural abilities tied to specific yaku. Saki alone sparked a wave of Western interest; you can still find threads on r/Mahjong from people who picked up the game after watching it.

These shows weren't just entertainment. They were functional tutorials. Viewers learned the rules, the yaku, and the terminology by watching characters play. The "Akagi to Tenhou pipeline" (watch Akagi, get curious, start playing on Tenhou) became a well-known phenomenon in online mahjong communities.

Video Games

The Yakuza (Ryu ga Gotoku) series by Sega has included fully playable riichi mahjong minigames since Yakuza 2 (2006). For many Western players, Yakuza was their first exposure to mahjong. They'd stumble into a mahjong parlor while exploring Kamurocho, get destroyed by the AI (Yakuza's mahjong is notoriously difficult; the AI seems to read your hand), and emerge determined to figure out what just happened. The series' growing Western popularity (Yakuza 0 sold over 1 million copies outside Japan) brought riichi mahjong to an audience that never would have sought it out.

Final Fantasy XIV added a full riichi mahjong mode (Doman Mahjong) in the Shadowbringers expansion (2019). With over 30 million registered players, FFXIV became the single largest vector for mahjong exposure in the West. The game includes a complete tutorial, AI opponents, and matchmaking against other players, all inside an MMO.

Online Platforms

Mahjong Soul (Jantama) launched in 2018 and became the dominant riichi mahjong platform in the West. Its anime-style characters, gacha system, and polished mobile client turned mahjong into a daily habit for millions of players. In 2024, Mahjong Soul reported over 10 million registered users worldwide.

Tenhou remains the most competitive platform. No gacha, no characters, pure mahjong. It's the platform of choice for serious players and the de facto standard for organized online tournaments.

Esports and Organized Play

The World Riichi Championship (WRC) held its first event in 2014 and has run every 2–3 years since, drawing players from over 30 countries. M-League, a professional riichi mahjong league in Japan launched in 2018, streams matches with commentary and has built a dedicated international following. The European Mahjong Association (EMA) and the American Riichi Mahjong Association (ARMA) both run regular tournaments with standardized rules.

Chinese mahjong has no comparable competitive infrastructure outside China. The World Mahjong Organization runs Chinese Official (Guobiao) tournaments, but these are smaller, less frequent, and less visible to Western audiences.

The Bottom Line

If you're a Western player discovering mahjong in 2026, you're almost certainly discovering riichi mahjong. The combination of anime, video games, and polished online platforms has made riichi the default entry point for the English-speaking world. Chinese mahjong remains the variant of choice for players with family or cultural ties to Chinese communities, but riichi's online ecosystem is unmatched.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is harder to learn, riichi or Chinese mahjong?

Riichi, no contest. The yaku system, furiten rule, and scoring all add friction that Chinese mahjong skips entirely; expect a few weeks to feel comfortable with riichi versus a session or two for the basics of Chinese.

Can you use Chinese mahjong tiles for riichi mahjong?

Yes, as long as you have all 136 base tiles (three suits of 1–9, four winds, three dragons, no flowers or seasons). A Chinese set works fine for learning and casual play. The only things you'd miss are the four red five tiles (aka dora) that Japanese sets include; those are optional. If you're buying a set specifically for riichi, a Japanese set is the better choice, but it's not required.

Why does riichi mahjong use Japanese terms?

Riichi mahjong uses Japanese terms because the game was introduced to Japan in the early 20th century and developed its own distinct ruleset there. Terms like "riichi" (ready hand declaration), "pon" (triplet call), "kan" (quad call), and yaku names like "tanyao" and "pinfu" are Japanese pronunciations of Chinese concepts. The Japanese mahjong community standardized these terms, and they've been adopted internationally as the game spread through anime, video games, and organized play. Most riichi players worldwide use Japanese terminology regardless of their native language, partly because translation would fragment the ruleset and partly because the Japanese terms are short and punchy in a way English equivalents aren't. "Riichi" beats "ready-hand declaration bet" every time.

Which variant is more popular in the West?

Riichi mahjong has become the dominant variant in Western competitive and online play, driven by its appearance in anime (Akagi, Saki), video games (Yakuza series, Mahjong Soul, Final Fantasy XIV), and the rise of online platforms like Mahjong Soul and Tenhou. Chinese mahjong remains more popular in Chinese diaspora communities worldwide, with Hong Kong-style being the most common variant played in Chinatowns across North America and Europe. For a Western player with no family connection to mahjong, riichi is the most accessible entry point.

What are the main differences between riichi and Chinese mahjong scoring?

The main differences are: (1) Riichi uses a han/fu system where han (yaku count) and fu (mini-points) multiply to determine the score, with mangan caps at 5, 8, and 13+ han. (2) Chinese mahjong uses a simpler fan/points system where each fan (winning pattern) has a fixed point value, and there is no cap on maximum score. (3) Riichi's scoring is more granular and rewards defensive play, while Chinese mahjong's scoring is more straightforward and rewards aggressive hand-building. (4) Riichi has dora tiles (bonus han from revealed indicators). Chinese mahjong has no equivalent mechanic.
