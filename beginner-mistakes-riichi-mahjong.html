BEGINNER SURVIVAL GUIDE

10 Beginner Mistakes to Avoid in Riichi Mahjong

Most losses at the beginner level are not bad luck. They are predictable mistakes. Fix these ten and you will immediately start finishing higher in your games.

When I started playing riichi mahjong, I thought the path to winning was memorizing every yaku. I spent hours studying yakuman hands I would never complete. Meanwhile, I was declaring riichi on bad waits, dealing into obvious riichi hands, and calling pon on tiles that did nothing for my score. I was losing because of basic mistakes, not because I lacked exotic knowledge. This article is the list I wish someone had handed me on day one.

Mistake 1: Ignoring Defense Entirely

This is the single most expensive mistake beginners make. They treat mahjong like a race to build their own hand and ignore the fact that three other players are also trying to win. When an opponent declares riichi, they keep pushing a half-finished hand and then act surprised when they deal in.

Defense is not optional. It is half the game. In a typical match, you will not win most hands. Your job is to avoid paying for the hands other people win. Start by learning genbutsu: discard tiles that your opponent has already discarded. Those tiles are safe. From there, learn suji and kabe to find safer discards when genbutsu is not available.

Mistake 2: Declaring Riichi on Bad Waits

Reaching tenpai feels great. Declaring riichi feels even better. But riichi locks your hand, costs 1,000 points, and announces your threat to the table. If your wait is a tanki on a single honor tile or a penchan on the edge of a suit, you are paying for a bet that probably will not pay off.

Before you declare riichi, ask two questions. Is my wait good? Is my hand valuable enough to justify the commitment? If the answer to either is no, consider staying dama (silent tenpai). You can still win by ron or tsumo, and you keep the ability to improve your hand or fold.

Mistake 3: Calling Pon or Chii Too Often

Calls are fun. They make your hand look active. But every call opens your hand, reveals information to opponents, and reduces your flexibility. A closed hand can declare riichi. An open hand usually cannot.

Call pon or chii only when the call clearly advances your yaku plan or speeds up a valuable hand. Do not call just because you can. A common beginner pattern: calling chii to complete a 1-2-3 sequence on the edge of a suit, opening the hand for a weak yaku, and then having no way to increase value. That call probably cost more than it gained.

Mistake 4: Discarding Dora Tiles

The dora is the scoring multiplier of riichi mahjong. Each copy of the dora tile in your hand adds one han. A hand with two dora tiles and tanyao is worth 3 han. Without dora, it is worth 1 han. That is a massive difference.

Beginners often discard dora because they are focused on shape and do not notice the indicator. Always check the dora indicator before your first discard. If a tile in your hand is dora, keep it unless you have a very good reason to discard it. Dora turns weak hands into dangerous hands.

Mistake 5: Not Knowing Your Yaku Before Building

You cannot win without a yaku. It sounds obvious, but beginners constantly build hands without a clear yaku plan. They end up in tenpai with a hand that scores nothing. Then they have to awkwardly pivot, often too late.

By turn four, you should know what yaku you are aiming for. Is it tanyao? Pinfu? Yakuhai? Riichi? Pick one direction and build toward it. If the tiles do not cooperate, you can pivot, but you need a starting plan. For a full reference, see our yaku list.

Mistake 6: Chasing Impossible Hands

Everyone wants to hit a yakuman. Half-flush, seven pairs, four concealed triplets. These hands are exciting. They are also rare, and chasing them from a bad start is a fast way to lose. If your starting hand has tiles scattered across all suits and no obvious high-value path, do not force it.

Build a simple, fast hand instead. Tanyao plus riichi wins games. Pinfu plus tanyao wins games. Fancy hands win highlight reels. Consistent simple hands win matches.

Mistake 7: Fear of Folding

Folding feels like giving up. Beginners hate it. They want to fight for every hand. But folding is not giving up. It is recognizing that the risk of dealing in outweighs the chance of winning.

When an opponent declares riichi and your hand is 2-shanten or worse, fold. When your hand is low value and the board is dangerous, fold. When you are in first place and a big hand would not change your position much, fold. Points you do not lose are points you keep.

Mistake 8: Misreading the Dora Indicator

The dora indicator is the tile flipped over near the start of the round. The dora tile itself is the next tile in sequence. If the indicator is 5 of characters, the dora is 6 of characters. If the indicator is a green dragon, the dora is a red dragon.

Beginners constantly misread this. They think the indicator itself is the dora, or they forget that wind and dragon dora follow a fixed order. Misreading dora means misvaluing your hand. Double-check the indicator every round.

Mistake 9: Playing Too Fast

Online mahjong gives you a limited time to discard, but beginners often use far less than they should. They discard on instinct without considering the board, their yaku, or their opponents' discards.

Slow down. Every discard is a decision. Ask yourself: what am I building? What is the safest discard? What have my opponents discarded? What is the dora? You do not need to agonize, but you should have a reason for every tile you discard.

Mistake 10: Not Practicing Efficiently

Mindless play teaches you slowly. If you want to improve fast, practice with intention. Pick one skill and focus on it for a session. Study tile efficiency for ten hands. Practice folding for ten hands. Drill yaku recognition for ten hands.

The One Habit That Fixes Most Mistakes

Before every discard, say out loud or in your head: "What am I building, and what is dangerous?" This one question forces you to think about both offense and defense. It prevents auto-pilot discards, bad riichi calls, and reckless pushes.

Why it works: Most beginner mistakes happen on autopilot. A simple pre-discard check interrupts bad habits before they cost you points.

How to Correct These Mistakes

You do not need to fix all ten mistakes at once. Pick the one that is costing you the most points and focus on it for your next twenty hands. For most beginners, that is mistake number one: defense. Start folding against riichi. Stop pushing weak hands. You will see your results improve almost immediately.

After defense, work on riichi discipline. Stop declaring riichi on bad waits. Then focus on tile efficiency. Then dora awareness. One step at a time. Riichi mahjong is a deep game, but the gap between a beginner and an intermediate player is mostly these ten mistakes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the biggest mistake beginners make in riichi mahjong?

The biggest mistake is ignoring defense. Beginners focus only on their own hand and deal into opponents' winning hands. Learning to fold against riichi and read safe tiles will save more points than any offensive improvement.

Should beginners declare riichi as soon as they reach tenpai?

No. Declaring riichi locks your hand and costs 1,000 points. Beginners should stay dama (silent tenpai) when their wait is bad, their hand is already valuable, or the round is dangerous. Riichi is a tool, not a reflex.

How do I stop dealing into other players' hands?

Use genbutsu, which means discarding tiles your opponent has already discarded. Then learn suji and kabe defense to find safer discards. Most importantly, fold when an opponent declares riichi unless your own hand is nearly complete and valuable.

Is it bad to call pon or chii often?

Calling too often is a common beginner mistake. Every call opens your hand, reveals information, and reduces your defensive flexibility. Call when the call clearly advances your yaku plan or speeds up a valuable hand. Otherwise, stay closed.

How long does it take to stop making beginner mistakes?

Most players stop the biggest mistakes within a few weeks of focused practice. Playing regularly, reviewing losses, and using training tools like Mahjong Quiz can accelerate the process dramatically.

Final Thoughts

Every riichi mahjong player makes these mistakes at some point. The difference between a stuck beginner and a growing intermediate is not talent. It is awareness. Once you start noticing these patterns in your own play, you can correct them.

Fix one mistake at a time, and soon you will look back at your old play and wonder what you were thinking.
