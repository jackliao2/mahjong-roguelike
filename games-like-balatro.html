ROGUELIKE DECKBUILDER GUIDE

12 Games Like Balatro to Play in 2026

If you finished Balatro and need your next obsession, here are the best roguelike deckbuilders, dice-rollers, and card strategy games — including a free one you can play right now in your browser.

Balatro changed the conversation around card roguelikes. It took a familiar deck of 52 playing cards, layered on Joker synergies, and turned it into one of the most addictive loops of 2024. But after 200 hours, even the best runs end — and you start looking for the next thing. This guide rounds up 12 games like Balatro that scratch the same itch, from established classics to fresh indie twists.

We've ranked these by how closely they capture the Balatro feel: short runs, build-defining choices, run-ending stakes, and that "just one more run" pull. We've also flagged which ones are beginner-friendly versus which demand prior genre knowledge.

#1

Balatro

YEAR: 2024 DEV: LocalThunk GENRE: Card Roguelike

The game that started the conversation. Balatro strips poker down to its scoring hands — pairs, flushes, full houses, straight flushes — and asks you to play them against a escalating score target across eight antes. Between rounds, you shop for Jokers that fundamentally rewrite how your deck scores.

The genius is in the synergy web. A single Joker like "Blueprint" (copies the effect of the Joker to its right) can cascade into absurd combos that break the score counter. Runs feel less like playing cards and more like assembling a Rube Goldberg machine out of poker hands.

Why it's here: It's the reference point. If you haven't played it yet, start here. If you have, everything else on this list is your next stop.
#2

Slay the Spire

YEAR: 2019 DEV: MegaCrit Games GENRE: Deckbuilding Roguelike

The granddaddy of the modern deckbuilder. Slay the Spire sends you up a spire of increasingly nasty enemies, drafting cards from a randomized pool after each fight. Four characters — Ironclad, Silent, Defect, Watcher — each play so differently they feel like separate games.

What sets StS apart is how readable it is. Enemy intents are telegraphed, so every turn is a puzzle: "How do I deal 18 damage, block 12, and still set up next turn?" The skill ceiling is enormous — top players have win streaks in the hundreds on Ascension 20.

Why it's like Balatro: Same run-based structure, same build-defining drafting, same "I'll stop after one more" grip. Combat-focused rather than scoring-focused.
#3 FREE · NO DOWNLOAD

Mahjong Roguelike

YEAR: 2026 DEV: Mahjong Roguelike Team GENRE: Mahjong + Roguelike

Okay, we're biased — but hear us out. Mahjong Roguelike applies the Balatro formula to riichi (Japanese) mahjong. Instead of poker hands, you build a 14-tile mahjong hand and score it against yaku (winning patterns). Between rounds, you draft relics, unlock custom tiles, and pick new yaku to add to your scoring pool.

The interesting twist: mahjong has way more depth than poker. Where Balatro has about 10 scoring hands, riichi mahjong has 40+ yaku, and the tile interactions are richer. Tanyao (all simples 2-8), Pinfu (all-sequences), Riichi (ready bet), Yakuhai (dragon triplet) — each one is a build archetype the way Joker synergies are in Balatro.

It's also free, runs in your browser, and works on mobile. No download, no account, no install. Just open the page and play a run in 10-15 minutes.

Why it's like Balatro: Same short-run loop, same relic-drafting between rounds, same "what build do I go for this run?" decisions — but with mahjong's deeper pattern library instead of poker hands.

#4

Monster Train

YEAR: 2020 DEV: Shiny Shoe GENRE: Deckbuilding Roguelike

If Slay the Spire is the thoughtful older sibling, Monster Train is the hyperactive younger one. You defend a three-floor train from angels trying to extinguish your hellfire. Each turn, you play cards across all three floors simultaneously, setting up buff chains and last-ditch defenses.

The signature mechanic is clan mixing — you draft a primary and secondary clan at run start, creating 25+ combinations before you even see a card. The Abyssal Fragments DLC adds a fifth clan and a third alliance slot, pushing build variety into the stratosphere.

Why it's like Balatro: Faster runs than StS, more explosive combos, and the same "I broke the game" highs when a build comes online.
#5

Inscryption

YEAR: 2021 DEV: Daniel Mullins Games GENRE: Card Horror / Meta Fiction

Inscryption is harder to categorize because it's deliberately unstable. The opening act is a creepy cabin where a shadowed figure forces you to play cards for your life. The rules bend as you play — you'll sacrifice creatures, weight the deck with items found in the room, and uncover secrets the game explicitly doesn't want you to find.

Then act two happens. And act three. And the game you started is not the game you finish. To say more spoils it.

Why it's like Balatro: The card combat is genuinely great — bone economics, creature sacrifices, sigil transfers. But you're really here for the journey, not endless replayability.
#6

Dicey Dungeons

YEAR: 2019 DEV: Terry Cavanagh GENRE: Dice Roguelike

From the creator of VVVVVV comes a roguelike where your deck is a handful of dice. Each turn you roll dice, then slot them into equipment cards — a sword needs a 3+ for damage, a shield wants even numbers, a poison flask multiplies whatever you feed it. Six classes play wildly differently, and the inventory limit forces tough cuts every floor.

The genius move: dice rolls are visible before you commit them, so it's a puzzle of optimization rather than pure luck. Bad rolls become interesting problems instead of failures.

Why it's like Balatro: Same compact run length, same equipment-as-Jokers synergy hunting, but with dice instead of cards.
#7

Ring of Pain

YEAR: 2020 DEV: Simon Boxer GENRE: Card Roguelike

A darker, more atmospheric take on the card roguelike. All cards — enemies, items, equipment — exist in a literal ring around you. Moving clockwise reveals the next card; counterclockwise retreats from threats. Combat is automatic based on your stats, so the gameplay is about positioning and item synergies rather than active card play.

It's shorter and tighter than StS — runs clock in around 30-40 minutes — with a focus on equipment builds that compound over a run.

Why it's like Balatro: Quick runs, build-centric item drafting, and the same slot-machine satisfaction of seeing a build come together.
#8

Dungeon Clawler

YEAR: 2024 DEV: Stray Fawn Studio GENRE: Claw Machine Roguelike

Yes, really — a claw machine roguelike. You physically aim a claw to grab tiles from a pit, and what you grab becomes your build. It sounds like a gimmick, but the execution is excellent: tiles have synergies, the pit layout is procedurally generated, and risk-reward decisions (go for the rare tile at the edge, or grab three commons in the center?) drive every turn.

A great pick if you want something physically tactile in a genre dominated by abstract card play.

Why it's like Balatro: Same run-based item hunting, but the claw mechanic adds a skill component that pure card-drafting lacks.
#9

Griftlands

YEAR: 2021 DEV: Klei Entertainment GENRE: Deckbuilding RPG

From the makers of Don't Starve comes a deckbuilder with a story. Griftlands runs three character campaigns where you build two decks in parallel — one for combat, one for negotiation. Fights you can't win with fists can sometimes be won with words, and the writing is genuinely good.

Longer and more narrative than Balatro — runs are 3-5 hours — but the deckbuilding core is rock-solid and the negotiation system is a genuine innovation.

Why it's like Balatro: Less directly — this is a story-driven RPG — but the draft-and-build loop will feel familiar.
#10

Luck be a Landlord

YEAR: 2021 DEV: TrampolineTales GENRE: Slot Machine Roguelike

The closest spiritual sibling to Balatro on this list. You're a tenant whose rent is paid by a slot machine. Each spin produces rent money; between spins, you buy new symbols and modifiers that interact with each other. Adjacency matters — a "Witch" hexes adjacent symbols, a "Mine" replaces neighbors with ores, a "Chef" doubles food-symbol payouts.

The floor is rising rent. The ceiling is your ingenuity in assembling a self-sustaining symbol engine. Sound familiar?

Why it's like Balatro: Almost identical DNA. If Balatro is "poker but a roguelike," this is "slots but a roguelike." Same dopamine, different skin.
#11

Cobalt Core

YEAR: 2023 DEV: Rocket Rat Games GENRE: Deckbuilding Roguelike

A single-player deckbuilder with a positioning twist — you pilot a ship on a 2D plane, and your cards move you forward, back, up, or down while firing weapons. Positioning matters as much as card choice: a perfectly timed dodge can negate an entire enemy volley.

From the team behind the Slay the Spire mods community, so the design pedigree is impeccable. Eight pilots, six ships, and 600+ cards mean the build variety is enormous.

Why it's like Balatro: Captures the "this run is special" feeling of breaking a build wide open — just with ship combat instead of poker hands.
#12

Wildfrost

YEAR: 2023 DEV: Deadpan Games GENRE: Deckbuilding Roguelike

A charming, surprisingly deep deckbuilder with a timing-based combat system. Cards have counters instead of immediate effects — you set up a turn, then enemies act. The appeal is in the charm: a cast of cuddly companions, a sun-soaked art style, and bosses named things like "Frost Guardian."

Don't let the cute exterior fool you — this is one of the harder deckbuilders on the list. The counter system means you're always planning two turns ahead, and one miscalculation can end a run.

Why it's like Balatro: Compact runs, build-defining companion drafting, and an accessible surface hiding real depth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What game is most similar to Balatro?

If we're talking pure mechanics, Luck be a Landlord is the closest match — same slot-machine-meets-roguelike loop with adjacency-based synergies. If you want the same deckbuilder feel but with more strategic depth, Slay the Spire is the gold standard. For a free browser alternative, Mahjong Roguelike applies the Balatro formula to riichi mahjong.

Are there any free games like Balatro?

Yes. Mahjong Roguelike is completely free and runs in any modern browser — no download or account required. It's the closest free equivalent to Balatro's run-based drafting loop, just with mahjong hands instead of poker hands. Several other entries on this list (Dicey Dungeons, Ring of Pain) also go on sale frequently for under $5.

Is Balatro on mobile?

Yes — Balatro launched on iOS and Android in September 2024 for $9.99. Slay the Spire, Monster Train, and Dicey Dungeons also have mobile ports. Mahjong Roguelike is browser-based and works on mobile browsers without an app install.

What makes a game "like Balatro"?

Three core ingredients: (1) short, repeatable runs of 15-45 minutes, (2) build-defining drafting between rounds where you pick upgrades that synergize with each other, and (3) escalating difficulty that forces you to commit to a build strategy. Any game with all three will give you the Balatro feeling, regardless of whether it uses cards, dice, or mahjong tiles.

Which of these is best for total beginners?

Mahjong Roguelike (free, browser) and Wildfrost (paid) are the most beginner-friendly. Both teach their mechanics gradually without a tutorial wall. Slay the Spire and Balatro are also accessible, but expect to lose your first 5-10 runs while learning the card pool.

What's the difference between Balatro and Slay the Spire?

Balatro is scoring-focused — you play poker hands to hit score targets, with no enemy AI. Slay the Spire is combat-focused — you play cards to defeat enemies with telegraphed attacks. Both are deckbuilders with run-based progression, but the moment-to-moment gameplay is very different. Fans of one usually enjoy the other.

How We Picked These Games

Every game on this list was evaluated against four criteria:

  • Run structure — Does it have short, repeatable runs with clear beginnings and endings?
  • Build variety — Can you pursue meaningfully different strategies across runs?
  • Drafting loop — Is there a between-rounds phase where you pick upgrades that synergize?
  • "One more run" pull — Does it have that hard-to-quantify quality where you keep saying "just one more"?

We deliberately included a mix of paid AAA-indie titles (Slay the Spire, Inscryption), mid-priced indies (Monster Train, Cobalt Core), and one free browser option (Mahjong Roguelike) so there's something here regardless of your budget or platform.
