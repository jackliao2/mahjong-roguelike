TENPAI FUNDAMENTALS

Waiting Tiles Explained: How to Read Mahjong Tenpai Hands

The moment your hand reaches tenpai, the game changes. You are no longer building. You are waiting. Learn how to read waits, pick the best one, and finish hands faster.

I remember the first time I reached tenpai in a real game. I had four melds lined up, one pair ready, and I was staring at a 2-3 of bamboo waiting on a 1 or 4. I had no idea if that was good or bad. I just knew I needed one more tile. What I did not understand then is that not all waits are equal. Some waits give you eight chances to win. Others give you one. Learning the difference is the first step toward reading mahjong hands like a skilled player.

What Is a Waiting Tile?

In riichi mahjong, a hand needs 14 tiles to win: four melds plus one pair. When you have 13 tiles and you are one tile away from a complete hand, you are in tenpai. The tile or tiles that would complete your hand are called your waiting tiles. If any opponent discards one of your waiting tiles, you can call ron and win the hand. If you draw one yourself, you can call tsumo and win.

Every winning hand ends with a wait. That means every decision you make during a round should eventually lead to a strong one. You can build the most beautiful hand in the world, but if your final wait is a single edge tile that nobody discards, you will watch someone else win while you sit on a near-complete hand.

The goal of this guide is simple: teach you to see waits clearly, name them correctly, and choose shapes that give you the most chances to win. If you are brand new to mahjong, start with our how-to-play guide first. Then come back here once you understand the basic rules of tenpai and yaku.

The Five Common Wait Types

Most waits in riichi mahjong fall into five categories. Learn these five and you will recognize almost every tenpai hand you encounter.

Ryanmen: The Two-Sided Wait

A ryanmen wait happens when you have two consecutive numbered tiles and you are waiting on either end to complete a sequence. A 4-5 of characters waits on 3 or 6 of characters. A 2-3 of circles waits on 1 or 4 of circles. This is the best wait in the game because it accepts eight tiles total: four copies of each of the two waiting tiles.

Ryanmen waits are fast, flexible, and often safe to push. They are also a requirement for pinfu, one of the most common yaku. If your hand can end in a ryanmen wait, it usually should. When you study tile efficiency, you are mostly learning how to shape your hand so that your final wait is ryanmen.

Kanchan: The Closed Wait

A kanchan wait happens when you have two numbered tiles with a gap in the middle and you are waiting on the middle tile. A 4-6 of bamboo waits on 5 of bamboo. A 2-4 of characters waits on 3 of characters. This wait only accepts four tiles, which makes it half as fast as ryanmen.

Kanchan waits are common during hand building because they often appear naturally. The key skill is recognizing when to keep a kanchan shape and when to break it. In general, keep kanchan early in the hand when you have time to improve it. Late in the hand, a kanchan tenpai is still tenpai, and tenpai is better than one-shanten. Just do not expect it to win quickly.

Penchan: The Edge Wait

A penchan wait happens at the edge of a suit. A 1-2 of characters waits only on 3 of characters. An 8-9 of circles waits only on 7 of circles. Like kanchan, this wait accepts only four tiles, but it is even worse because one of the possible completion tiles does not exist. You cannot complete a sequence below 1 or above 9.

Penchan waits are weak. If you can avoid ending on a penchan wait, do so. Sometimes they are unavoidable, especially when you are chasing a specific yaku. But as a beginner, your default goal should be to turn penchan waits into ryanmen waits by adjusting the shape of your hand.

Tanki: The Single-Tile Wait

A tanki wait means you are waiting on a single tile to complete your pair. This happens in hands where you have four complete melds and one lone tile. You are waiting on the second copy of that lone tile. Tanki only accepts three tiles in the wall, since you already hold one copy yourself.

Tanki waits are slow but sometimes necessary. In seven pairs, every wait is tanki. In a normal four-melds hand, you might fall into tanki if your last incomplete group is a pair. Skilled players sometimes choose tanki deliberately because it can be hard for opponents to read, but for beginners it is usually a sign that the hand became inefficient.

Shanpon: The Double-Pair Wait

A shanpon wait happens when you have two different pairs and you are waiting on either pair to become a triplet. Whichever pair completes becomes your meld, and the other pair becomes your final pair. This wait accepts six tiles total: three copies of each pair tile, since you already hold two of each.

Shanpon is better than tanki because it gives you two different winning tiles. It is not as good as ryanmen, but it is flexible and can sometimes lead to high-value hands if the pairs are valuable tiles like dragons or your seat wind.

Count the Winning Tiles

Whenever you reach tenpai, count how many tiles are left in the wall that could complete your hand. A ryanmen wait gives you eight. Kanchan and penchan give you four. Tanki gives you three. Shanpon gives you six. The higher the number, the faster your hand is likely to win.

Why it matters: A hand with eight winning tiles will complete twice as fast, on average, as a hand with four. In a race against three opponents, that difference is enormous.

How to Read a Tenpai Hand

Reading a tenpai hand means identifying the incomplete meld and the tile that completes it. This is the core skill tested in our waiting-tiles quiz mode. Here is the process.

First, group the hand into four melds and one pair. Look for completed sequences and triplets. Those are your locked blocks. Then look at what is left. Usually you will find one incomplete group: either a pair needing a third tile, a two-tile sequence needing a third, or two pairs in a shanpon shape.

Second, identify the waiting tile. If the incomplete group is a pair, you need the third copy. If it is a two-tile sequence, you need the tile that completes the run. If it is two pairs, you need either tile to become a triplet.

Third, count how many tiles could win. If the incomplete sequence is 4-5 of circles, you are waiting on 3 or 6 of circles. That is a ryanmen wait with eight winning tiles. If the incomplete sequence is 1-2 of characters, you are waiting only on 3 of characters. That is a penchan wait with four winning tiles.

With practice, this becomes automatic. You will look at a hand and instantly see the waits without counting. Our quiz mode gives you unlimited practice on real hand shapes, which is the fastest way to build that intuition.

Multi-Wait Shapes

Some advanced hands wait on more than one tile in complex ways. These are called multi-wait shapes, and they are some of the most powerful positions in mahjong. The classic example is a 2-3-3-4 shape of a suit. Depending on how you group the tiles, this shape can wait on 1, 2, 4, or 5 of that suit.

Multi-wait shapes usually involve overlapping sequences or pairs that can be grouped in more than one way. They are harder to read but worth studying because they dramatically increase your chance of winning. If you enjoy puzzles, multi-wait questions are the most satisfying part of mahjong training.

Do not worry if multi-wait shapes feel confusing at first. Master the five basic waits first. Once you can instantly recognize ryanmen, kanchan, penchan, tanki, and shanpon, multi-wait shapes become much easier to break down.

Why Waits Matter for Yaku

Your wait is not just about speed. It can also affect your yaku. Pinfu requires a ryanmen wait. Riichi gives you access to ura-dora, which can multiply your score. A dama hand with a good wait lets you win silently without committing to riichi.

Some yaku change which wait you want. Seven pairs uses only tanki waits, so you stop worrying about ryanmen entirely. A half-flush hand might accept a worse wait because completing the suit is more important than perfect efficiency. Always think about your yaku plan before you decide whether a wait is good or bad.

Practical Training Tips

The fastest way to improve at reading waits is deliberate practice. Here are three exercises that work.

  • Drill the basics: Deal yourself 13 tiles, find the one discard that gives you the best wait, and verify your answer. Repeat until you can do it in under 10 seconds.
  • Use quiz mode: Our Mahjong Quiz has dedicated waiting-tiles questions that show you a tenpai hand and ask what it is waiting on. This is the most efficient way to train pattern recognition.
  • Count winning tiles: Every time you reach tenpai in a real game, mentally count how many tiles can complete your hand. This habit will make you more aware of wait quality.

Waiting tiles are not just a detail. They are the finish line of every hand you play. Learn to see them clearly and you will start winning more often without memorizing a single new yaku.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best type of wait in riichi mahjong?

Ryanmen (two-sided wait) is the best wait type. A sequence like 4-5 of a suit accepts both 3 and 6, giving you eight winning tiles. This is twice as many as a kanchan (closed wait), which only accepts four tiles. Ryanmen waits are faster, more flexible, and harder for opponents to defend against.

How do I know which tile my hand is waiting on?

Group your hand into four melds and one pair. The missing piece of the incomplete meld is your waiting tile. If you have a 6-7 of characters, you are waiting on 5 or 8 of characters. If you have a pair of 3 of circles and need a triplet, you are waiting on the third 3 of circles. Practice with our Mahjong Quiz waiting-tiles questions to build this skill quickly.

Can a mahjong hand wait on more than one tile?

Yes. Many tenpai hands wait on multiple tiles. A ryanmen wait accepts two different tiles, and some advanced shapes can wait on three or even more tiles at once. These multi-wait hands are valuable because they give you more chances to win before another player completes their hand.

What is the difference between tanki and shanpon waits?

A tanki wait means you are waiting on a single tile to complete your pair. A shanpon wait means you have two different pairs and you are waiting on either tile to become a triplet, which leaves the other pair as your final pair. Shanpon is generally better than tanki because it gives you two winning tiles instead of one.

Should I always aim for a ryanmen wait?

Not always. Ryanmen is ideal, but sometimes a kanchan or tanki wait is the fastest path to tenpai. Also, some yaku require specific waits. Pinfu requires a ryanmen wait, while seven pairs uses only tanki waits. Choose the wait that fits your hand's yaku plan and the current game state.

Final Thoughts

Waiting tiles are where offense meets opportunity. A good player builds hands. A strong player builds hands that end in strong waits. The difference is not luck. It is pattern recognition, and pattern recognition comes from practice.
