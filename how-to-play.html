SECTION 02

Forming a Hand

A complete mahjong hand is 14 tiles arranged as 4 melds plus 1 pair. Each player starts with 13 tiles and draws one per turn, then discards one — you win by drawing (or being dealt) the 14th tile that completes the shape.

The Three Meld Types

A meld is a group of 3 tiles. There are three kinds:

SEQUENCE (CHII) — 3 consecutive tiles, same suit

There's one important exception: the seven pairs yaku (chiitoitsu) lets you win with 7 unrelated pairs instead of the standard 4 melds + pair. See the yaku list for details.

The 14-tile rule: When you have 13 tiles and are waiting for one more to win, you are in tenpai (ready). When you actually have all 14, you have agari (a win).

SECTION 03

Winning Patterns (Yaku)

In riichi mahjong, you can't just complete any hand — your winning hand must contain at least one yaku: a recognized winning pattern. Each yaku is worth a number of han, and you can stack multiple yaku in the same hand for big scores.

Here are three yaku every beginner should learn first:

Riichi — 1 han

Declare "ready" when one tile away from winning. Your hand locks in, but you earn a guaranteed yaku.

Tanyao — 1 han

All your tiles are 2-8. No terminals (1s, 9s) and no honors. The easiest "open" yaku.

Pinfu — 1 han

All four melds are sequences, with a two-sided wait for the winning tile. Pure and clean.

There are dozens more — from Yakuhai (a triplet of dragons or your seat wind) up to the legendary Kokushi Musou (thirteen orphans). Browse the full list with han values and examples on the yaku encyclopedia.

SECTION 04

Scoring Basics

Your final score comes from two numbers: han (pattern difficulty) and fu (hand structure value). Together they determine your point total.

Han — Pattern Value

Each yaku is worth 1 to 6 han. Multiple yaku add together. At 5 han you reach haneman, and beyond that special tier names kick in.

Fu — Structure Value

Fu measures how "complex" your melds are. Triplets are worth more than sequences. Closed triplets are worth more than open ones. Honor triplets are worth the most.

Base: 20 fu

Every winning hand starts at 20 fu just for winning.

Sequences: 0 fu

Sequences add no fu — they're the cheapest meld.

Triplets: 4-32 fu

Depends on open/closed and whether it's a simple, terminal, or honor tile.

Pair: 0-4 fu

Dragon pairs and seat-wind pairs add 2 fu each.

The shortcut: Don't worry about calculating fu by hand at first. The game handles scoring for you. What matters is recognizing yaku and stacking them — the more yaku, the higher your score climbs.

Riichi Mahjong FAQ

Is riichi mahjong hard to learn?

The basic rules can be learned in 20-30 minutes: form 4 melds plus a pair, declare riichi when ready, and recognize 3-4 easy yaku. Mastery takes longer, but you can play your first competent game within an hour of reading this guide. The yaku system is the main learning curve — once you know the 16 common yaku, you're set.

How long does a game of riichi mahjong take?

A full hanchan (East + South rounds) takes 60-90 minutes with four players. A tonpusou (East-only) game takes 30-45 minutes. In our browser roguelike, individual rounds take 10-15 minutes — perfect for a coffee break.

What is the difference between riichi mahjong and Chinese mahjong?

Riichi mahjong (Japanese) adds the riichi declaration (a 1,000-point bet that you're one tile from winning), a richer yaku system with ~40 patterns, dora bonus tiles, and a more restrictive scoring system that rewards closed hands. Chinese mahjong variants tend to allow more open melds and have simpler scoring with fewer patterns.

Do I need to memorize all the yaku?

No. You can play competently knowing just 4 easy yaku: Riichi, Tanyao, Pinfu, and Yakuhai. The rest come naturally with experience. Bookmark our complete yaku list and refer to it between rounds — in our browser game, the score panel shows which yaku your hand scored, so you learn by doing.

What does "riichi" mean?

Riichi (立直, "ready") is the signature mechanic of Japanese mahjong. When your hand is one tile away from winning (a state called tenpai), you can declare riichi by paying a 1,000-point deposit. Your hand locks in — you can no longer change it — but you gain a guaranteed yaku, access to ippatsu (one-draw win bonus), and ura-dora (hidden dora bonuses if you win).

Read this guide once to understand the tiles and rules. Then play immediately — even badly. Theory only sticks once you've held tiles in your hand.
