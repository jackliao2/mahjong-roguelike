STRATEGY GUIDE

Riichi Mahjong Strategy: 10 Tips to Win More Hands

You've learned the rules and you know the yaku. Now it's time to actually win. These 10 strategy tips will take you from losing half your hands to consistently threatening the top of the table.

The gap between knowing the rules of riichi mahjong and winning consistently is wider than most players expect. You can memorize every yaku, understand the scoring system, and still lose hand after hand because you're missing the strategic layer that sits between "knowing what's legal" and "knowing what's good." This guide is for players who have moved past the tutorial phase and are ready to play intentional mahjong — where every discard, every call, and every riichi decision is backed by a reason. Master these 10 tips and you'll stop wondering why you lost and start knowing why you won.

Before we dive in, a quick note: these tips are arranged from foundational to advanced. Tips 1-4 cover the fundamentals that every intermediate player needs. Tips 5-7 introduce the efficiency concepts that separate good players from great ones. Tips 8-10 cover the defensive and psychological layers that define high-level play. If you're brand new to riichi mahjong, start with our beginner's guide first, then come back here.

Tip 1: Master the 5-Block Theory

Every winning mahjong hand is composed of five blocks: four melds (either sequences or triplets) plus one pair. This is the fundamental organizing principle of hand building, and internalizing it will transform how you see your tiles.

When you look at your starting hand, don't just see 13 random tiles. Mentally group them into candidate blocks. A pair of 3-sou is one block. A 4-man 5-man is a partial block (one tile away from a sequence). A lone 1-pin sitting by itself is not a block at all — it's dead weight. Count your blocks. If you have more than five, you have choices. If you have fewer than five, you need to build.

The 5-block framework answers the most common beginner question: "which tile do I discard?" The answer is almost always: the tile that belongs to your weakest or most redundant block. If you have six candidate blocks, break the weakest one. If you have four solid blocks and one partial, focus on completing the partial. This is the lens through which every discard decision should be filtered.

A practical exercise: take your next 10 starting hands and physically count the blocks. You'll quickly notice patterns. Starting hands with four or fewer blocks are slow — you need to draw aggressively. Hands with five or six blocks are fast — your job is to refine, not to build. This simple mental model will improve your early-game decision making more than any other single concept.

KEY INSIGHT

Block Quality Matters

Not all blocks are equal. A pair of 3-sou and a 3-sou-4-sou partial sequence are both blocks, but the partial sequence is far stronger because it can accept two different tiles (2-sou or 5-sou) to complete. A pair only has one path to completion. When choosing which block to discard, prioritize block quality — the number of tiles that can complete it — over everything else.

Beginner mistake: Holding onto honor tiles (winds and dragons) as blocks. Unless you have a pair or a clear path to yakuhai, honor tiles are not blocks — they're dead tiles. Discard them early.

Tip 2: Always Know Your Yaku Before Building

This sounds obvious, but it's the most violated rule in intermediate play. Walk into a mahjong parlor and you'll see players collecting tiles with no clear plan — they're building a hand that looks complete but has no valid yaku, so it's worthless. You cannot win without a yaku, and you cannot win with a hand you built backward.

By the third or fourth discard, you should have identified at least one viable yaku for your hand. Are your tiles mostly numbered 2-8? Tanyao (all simples) is your plan. Do you have a pair of dragons and a third one already? Yakuhai (dragon triplet) is your entry point. Is your hand all sequences with no honor tiles? Pinfu is calling your name. A hand that is closed and nearly complete? Riichi is always available as a fallback.

The key is to commit early. The worst thing you can do is hold onto tiles for two different yaku, hoping one of them materializes. That's like trying to merge two half-built houses — you end up with a pile of materials and no roof. Pick a direction by turn 4, and if the tiles don't cooperate, you can pivot — but you must have a direction to pivot from.

Here's a concrete framework: when you look at your hand after the first few draws, ask yourself three questions in order: (1) What yaku can I reasonably aim for? (2) Which tiles in my hand don't contribute to that yaku? (3) Which of those tiles should I discard first? If you can't answer question 1, your hand is in trouble and you should start thinking about defense. If you can answer it, questions 2 and 3 become mechanical.

KEY INSIGHT

The Yaku Ladder

Think of yaku as a ladder, not a menu. Your job isn't to pick the "best" yaku — it's to find the highest rung your tiles can reach. Tanyao and yakuhai are the bottom rungs: easy to achieve, low value. Riichi, pinfu, and iipeikou are the middle rungs: solid value, moderate difficulty. Chinitsu, honitsu, and toitoi are the top rungs: high value, but they demand specific tile compositions. Climb as high as your tiles allow, but don't fall off the ladder trying to reach a rung that isn't there.

Beginner mistake: Aiming for a high-value yaku (like chinitsu — a full flush) from a hand that has no business attempting it. A 1-han tanyao win is infinitely better than a 6-han chinitsu that never completes.

Tip 3: Defense Is Half the Game

Many players treat riichi mahjong as a four-player race to tenpai. It's not. It's a game where three-quarters of the time, someone else will win. Your job is to make sure that someone else isn't the person you deal into. In a typical hanchan (East-South game), the difference between a winning player and a losing player is often not how many hands they win, but how many hands they avoid paying for.

Defense starts with reading the discard pile. When an opponent discards a tile, that tile and its neighbors become safer for you to discard. This is the genbutsu principle: a tile an opponent has already discarded cannot be part of their winning hand (with rare exceptions like kokushi musou). If you see a player discard a 5-pin, you know that 5-pin is 100% safe to discard against that player. This is the most reliable defensive tool in the game.

But defense isn't just about individual safe tiles. It's about reading the board state. When a player declares riichi, they're telling you they're one tile away from winning. Your default response should be to fold — stop trying to build your hand and switch entirely to defensive discards. The only exception is if your hand is also in tenpai with good value and a good wait, which brings us to the push-fold decision (covered in Tip 9).

Even before riichi is declared, you should be watching for danger signs: a player who has discarded very few tiles from one suit is likely building toward a flush. A player who suddenly starts discarding tiles from a suit they were previously collecting may have reached tenpai. A player who calls pon on a dragon early is building value and should be treated with caution. These signals are subtle, but they're the difference between paying 12,000 points and paying zero.

KEY INSIGHT

The One-Han Rule

Here's a mental model that will save you thousands of points: assume every opponent in tenpai has at least one han. A one-han hand with a riichi stick is worth roughly 1,300-2,000 points on a ron (depending on the dealer/non-dealer). If your hand is not in tenpai and you're facing a riichi declaration, ask yourself: is the potential gain from continuing to build worth the near-certain loss of 2,000 points? The answer is almost always no.

Beginner mistake: Continuing to push a hand that's 2-shanten (two tiles from tenpai) against a player who has declared riichi. The math is brutal: they're one tile from winning, you're three tiles from even being ready. Fold.

Tip 4: When to Declare Riichi (and When NOT to)

Riichi is the most iconic mechanic in Japanese mahjong, and it's also the most misused. Declaring riichi costs 1,000 points, locks your hand, and announces to the entire table that you're one tile from winning. For that price, you get one additional han, access to ura-dora, and the psychological pressure of a declared threat. The decision to riichi or stay dama (silent tenpai) is one of the most important strategic choices you'll make in any hand.

When to declare riichi: Your hand is low-value and needs the extra han. You're in the first 5-8 turns of the round, so opponents haven't had time to build safe hands. Your wait is good — ideally a ryanmen (two-sided) wait that accepts 8 tiles. You're the dealer and want to pressure the table. The riichi stick is a bet that you'll win the hand, and these are the conditions where the bet pays off.

When to stay dama: Your hand is already valuable without riichi (3+ han from other yaku). The extra han from riichi adds proportionally less to your score, and the risk of dealing into someone else's hand increases. Your wait is bad — a tanki (single tile), kanchan (closed wait), or penchan (edge wait) means you're unlikely to draw the winning tile yourself. You're in the late game (turn 10+) and multiple opponents have shown signs of tenpai. Staying dama lets you fold if a dangerous tile appears, which riichi explicitly prevents.

The most painful thing in mahjong is declaring riichi with a bad wait, watching opponents fold, and then dealing into someone else's tsumo because you're forced to draw and discard every turn. That 1,000-point stick becomes a 1,000-point donation to the winner. If you're going to riichi, make sure your wait justifies the commitment.

KEY INSIGHT

The Dama Advantage

Staying dama isn't just about avoiding risk — it's a strategic weapon. A dama tenpai hand can still win by ron (calling an opponent's discard), and opponents are far more likely to discard your winning tile when they don't know you're tenpai. A dama hand with a good wait can surprise-win against players who assumed they were safe. The riichi declaration is a sledgehammer; dama is a scalpel. Know which tool fits the situation.

Beginner mistake: Riichi-ing every single time you reach tenpai. Riichi is a tool, not a reflex. Ask yourself: "Does this hand need the riichi han to be worth winning?" If the answer is no, seriously consider dama.

Tip 5: Tile Efficiency 101

Tile efficiency is the engine that drives everything else. It's the mathematical core of hand building: for any given hand state, which discard maximizes the number of tiles that bring you closer to tenpai? If you master nothing else on this list, master tile efficiency. It's the single highest-leverage skill in riichi mahjong.

The core concept is ukeire — tile acceptance. For each possible discard, count how many tiles would improve your hand's shanten number (how many tiles away you are from tenpai). The discard with the highest ukeire count is usually the correct one. Here's a concrete example: you have a 2-man 3-man 4-man 5-man 6-man sequence. You need to discard one. If you discard the 2-man, you can still draw 1-man, 4-man, or 7-man to complete a sequence. If you discard the 3-man, your acceptance drops. The 2-man and 6-man are the "edge" tiles of the run — they're the correct discards.

Some key efficiency principles to memorize:

  • Ryanmen (two-sided) waits are king. A 4-5 sequence accepts 3 and 6 — eight tiles total. A 4-6 kanchan (closed wait) only accepts 5 — four tiles. That's a 2x difference in completion speed. Prioritize shaping your hand toward ryanmen waits.
  • Floating tiles near the center (3-7) are more valuable than edge tiles (1-2, 8-9). A lone 5 can form sequences with 3, 4, 6, or 7 — that's four possible partners. A lone 1 can only form sequences with 2 or 3. When you have to discard a floating tile, discard from the edge.
  • Pairs are more valuable than isolated tiles. A pair is already a complete block. An isolated tile is nothing. Protect your pairs.
  • Completed melds (triplets or sequences) are locked value. Don't break them unless you have a very specific reason.

You don't need to do exact ukeire calculations during a live game. With practice, the patterns become intuitive. You'll start to feel which discards "open up" the hand and which ones "close it down." That's tile efficiency at work.

Tip 6: Read the Dora

The dora indicator is the most overlooked tile on the table. Beginners glance at it once and forget it. Intermediate players check it occasionally. Expert players build their entire strategy around it. The dora system is riichi mahjong's most powerful scoring multiplier, and ignoring it is like playing poker without checking if you have a flush draw.

Here's the rule: the tile after the dora indicator (in sequence order) is worth one additional han per copy in your hand. If the dora indicator is a 3-pin, then every 4-pin in your hand is worth one han. If you have three 4-pins, that's three han — the equivalent of having a full additional yaku. The dora is not a yaku itself (you still need a valid yaku to win), but it turbocharges the value of any hand that contains it.

The strategic implications are profound:

  • Prioritize keeping dora tiles. A hand with two dora tiles and a tanyao is worth 3 han. The same hand without dora is worth 1 han. That's the difference between a 1,000-point hand and a 3,900-point hand. Always check the dora indicator before you start discarding.
  • Defend against dora. If an opponent is collecting a suit that contains the dora, their hand's value is higher than it appears. Treat them with extra caution.
  • Ura-dora is the hidden multiplier. When you declare riichi and win, you reveal the tile underneath the dora indicator. Every tile matching the ura-dora indicator's successor is an additional han. A riichi hand with good dora alignment can explode from 2 han to 6+ han. This is why riichi is so powerful — and why you should think about dora alignment before declaring.
  • Kan (quad) calls add new dora indicators. Every kan adds a new dora, which can dramatically shift the value landscape. Think carefully before calling kan when an opponent is in tenpai — you might be handing them extra han.
KEY INSIGHT

Dora Alignment

When deciding whether to declare riichi, check your hand's dora alignment. If you have one or more dora tiles in your hand, and your wait is on a tile that could be dora or ura-dora, your potential payout is significantly higher than the base value suggests. Conversely, if you have zero dora and your wait is on a generic tile, your riichi is a gamble — you're betting 1,000 points that the ura-dora will bail you out.

Beginner mistake: Discarding dora tiles early because they don't fit your current yaku plan. Dora tiles are worth pivoting for. Before you discard a dora, consider whether you can reshape your hand around it.

Tip 7: The Power of Pinfu

If riichi mahjong had a "training wheels" yaku that every serious player should master, it would be pinfu. Pinfu is the "all sequences" hand — four sequences plus a pair, with a ryanmen (two-sided) wait, and the pair cannot be a value wind or dragon. It's worth one han, and it's closed-hand only. On paper, it looks unremarkable. In practice, it's the most versatile yaku in the game.

Why pinfu is so powerful for developing players:

  • It teaches good hand-building habits. Pinfu forces you to build sequences, avoid honor tiles, and aim for two-sided waits — all of which are fundamental efficiency skills. If you can consistently build pinfu hands, you can build anything.
  • It combines with everything. Pinfu stacks with tanyao (all simples), iipeikou (pure double sequence), sanshoku (mixed triple sequence), and of course riichi. A riichi + pinfu + tanyao hand is 3 han before dora — a solid, consistent earner.
  • It's fast. Because pinfu requires only sequences, you're drawing from the widest possible pool of useful tiles. Pinfu hands reach tenpai faster than hands that rely on triplets or honor tiles.
  • It's flexible. A hand headed toward pinfu can pivot to other yaku if the tiles don't cooperate. Got a pair of dragons instead of a value pair? Pivot to yakuhai. Drew a third copy of a tile? Pivot to iipeikou. Pinfu is the "default mode" of efficient hand building.

The pinfu requirements are specific — memorize them: (1) four sequences, (2) a ryanmen wait, (3) a pair that is not a yakuhai pair (value wind or dragon). That third condition trips up a lot of players. If your pair is a round wind, seat wind, or dragon, you don't have pinfu — you have the yakuhai yaku instead (which is fine, but it changes the hand's identity).

For beginners, I recommend this exercise: play 10 hands where your goal is to build pinfu + tanyao. Don't worry about winning. Just focus on reaching tenpai with a pinfu-eligible hand. After 10 hands, you'll notice your tile efficiency has improved dramatically — because you've been practicing the core skill without realizing it.

Tip 8: Suji and Kabe Defense

Once you've internalized the genbutsu principle (discard what opponents have already discarded), the next layer of defense is suji and kabe. These are probabilistic defensive techniques — they don't guarantee safety, but they dramatically reduce the chance of dealing in.

Suji exploits the fact that most waits are ryanmen (two-sided). A ryanmen wait on a 4-5 sequence means the player needs a 3 or a 6. If a player has discarded a 1, it's likely (though not certain) that they don't have a 2-3 sequence waiting on a 4 — because if they had a 2-3, they probably wouldn't have discarded the 1. This creates a chain: if a player discards a 1, the 4 becomes safer. If they discard a 7, the 4 also becomes safer (because a 5-6 sequence waiting on 4 or 7 would be unlikely to discard the 7). The suji pairs are 1-4-7 and 2-5-8 and 3-6-9, and a discard in one position makes the other positions in its group safer.

Kabe (literally "wall") exploits the fact that you can see all four copies of some tiles. If you can see all four copies of a 3-pin (in your hand, in discards, and in open melds), then no opponent can have a 2-3 sequence waiting on a 1 or 4. The 3-pin is a "wall" — it blocks the 2-3 wait entirely. Similarly, if all four 7-sou are visible, the 8-9 wait is blocked, making the 6-sou and 9-sou safer. Kabe is the strongest defensive signal after genbutsu, because it's based on certainty rather than probability.

When facing a riichi declaration, use this defensive hierarchy:

  1. Genbutsu — tiles the riichi player has already discarded. 100% safe.
  2. Kabe-blocked tiles — tiles that cannot be part of a wait because all four copies of a blocking tile are visible. Nearly 100% safe.
  3. Suji — tiles that are unlikely to be part of a ryanmen wait based on the riichi player's discards. Safe against ryanmen, dangerous against other wait types.
  4. Everything else — assume it's dangerous unless you have a specific reason to believe otherwise.
KEY INSIGHT

Suji Is Probabilistic, Not Absolute

Suji only protects against ryanmen waits. It does not protect against tanki (single tile), kanchan (closed wait), penchan (edge wait), or shanpon (double pair) waits. Against a riichi declaration, suji is good — but not infallible. If you have a suji tile and a genbutsu tile, always discard the genbutsu first. Suji is your second line of defense, not your first.

Beginner mistake: Assuming suji tiles are 100% safe. They're not. A player waiting on a kanchan (e.g., 4-6 waiting on 5) ignores suji entirely. Suji reduces risk; it doesn't eliminate it.

Tip 9: Push vs Fold Decision Making

Every hand eventually reaches a decision point: do I continue pushing toward tenpai, or do I fold and play defense? This is the single most impactful decision in riichi mahjong, and it separates winning players from losing ones. The push-fold decision is a risk-reward calculation, and you can get better at it by making it explicit rather than intuitive.

Here's a framework for making the push-fold decision:

Push when:

  • Your hand is in tenpai with a good wait (ryanmen or better).
  • Your hand has 3+ han of value (including dora).
  • You're the dealer and winning the hand means an extra turn.
  • No opponent has declared riichi or shown signs of tenpai.
  • You're behind in points and need to make a move.

Fold when:

  • An opponent has declared riichi and your hand is 2-shanten or worse.
  • Your hand has low value (1 han) and the risk of dealing in is high.
  • You're in the lead and protecting your position is more important than extending it.
  • Multiple opponents are showing signs of tenpai (aggressive discards, open calls).
  • You're in the last few turns of the round and haven't reached tenpai.

The hardest case is when you're in tenpai with a decent hand but an opponent has declared riichi. This is the classic "push-fold dilemma." My rule of thumb: if your hand has 3+ han and a good wait, push. If your hand has 1 han or a bad wait, fold. If it's 2 han with a good wait — it depends on the turn number and your position, and you'll need to develop your own instincts for this gray zone.

One more thing: folding doesn't mean you've lost the hand. A folded hand that pays zero points is a net win compared to pushing and dealing in. Points you don't lose are points you keep. The best players fold more often than you think — because they understand that preserving position is a winning strategy over the long run.

Tip 10: Practice Makes Permanent

The final tip is the most important and the least glamorous: you need to play. A lot. Strategy articles and theory are valuable, but they're scaffolding — the real learning happens at the table, where you have 20 seconds to make a discard decision and the pressure is real. Here's how to practice effectively:

Start with the right tools. Online platforms like Tenhou and Mahjong Soul are free and have active player bases. They handle scoring, yaku validation, and rule enforcement automatically, so you can focus on strategy. Start in the beginner rooms and don't be discouraged by losses — the ELO system will find your level.

Practice with a purpose. Don't just play mindlessly. Pick one tip from this list and focus on it for an entire session. Play 10 hands where your only goal is perfect tile efficiency. Play 10 hands where your only goal is to fold correctly against riichi. Play 10 hands where you never declare riichi — dama only. Deliberate practice on a single skill will improve you faster than general play.

Review your losses. After every hand you lose, ask yourself: "Why did I lose?" Was it bad luck? (Sometimes yes, but not as often as we tell ourselves.) Or was it a decision you made — a push when you should have folded, a riichi with a bad wait, a yaku you chased that was never going to complete? Being honest about your mistakes is uncomfortable but essential.

It's not a substitute for playing against humans, but it's an excellent way to practice yaku recognition, tile efficiency, and hand building in a low-pressure environment. The roguelike format also teaches you to adapt to constraints — a skill that transfers directly to real play.

Most importantly, don't expect to win overnight. Riichi mahjong has a steep learning curve, and even strong players lose 60-70% of their hands. The goal isn't to win every hand — it's to win more hands than you lose, and to lose fewer points on the hands you don't win. That's the real measure of improvement.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important skill in riichi mahjong?

Tile efficiency is the single most important skill. It's the ability to maximize your hand's speed toward tenpai by discarding tiles with the fewest paths to completion. Good tile efficiency alone will carry you past beginner ranks. Once you've internalized efficiency, layer on defense and push-fold decision making. But efficiency comes first — it's the foundation every other skill builds on.

Should beginners always declare riichi?

No — and this is one of the most common bad habits new players develop. Riichi is powerful but it has real costs: you invest 1,000 points, you lock your hand, and you announce your tenpai to the table. Stay dama (silent tenpai) when your hand is already valuable without riichi, when your wait is bad, or when you're in a dangerous position. Riichi is a tool, not a reflex. Use it when it adds value, not just because you can.

How do I know which tile to discard?

Use the 5-block theory and tile acceptance counting. First, group your tiles into candidate blocks (four melds + one pair). If you have more than five blocks, discard from the weakest one. If you have five, compare the acceptance count of each possible discard — the tile that gives you the most paths to completion is usually correct. In the late game, factor in safety: avoid discarding tiles that are likely to deal into an opponent's hand. With practice, this becomes faster and more intuitive.

What is the best yaku for beginners to aim for?

Pinfu (all sequences) is the best yaku for beginners. It teaches you to build efficient, closed hands without relying on triplets or honor tiles. Tanyao (all simples, tiles 2-8) is another excellent beginner yaku because it's easy to achieve and opens up the hand for calling. Once you can consistently build toward pinfu and tanyao, add riichi to your repertoire. These three yaku — pinfu, tanyao, and riichi — will form the backbone of most winning hands at the beginner and intermediate level. For a complete reference, see our full yaku list.

How do I defend against opponents in riichi mahjong?

Defense operates on a hierarchy. First, discard tiles your opponent has already discarded (genbutsu) — these are 100% safe. Second, use kabe (wall) defense: if all four copies of a tile are visible, tiles that would require that tile to complete a wait become safe. Third, use suji: if an opponent discards a 1, the 4 becomes safer against ryanmen waits. When an opponent declares riichi, fold unless your hand is nearly complete with good value. The best defense is discarding tiles that cannot possibly be your opponent's winning tile. For a deeper dive, read our complete how-to-play guide.

Putting It All Together

Riichi mahjong strategy is a layered system. The 10 tips in this guide build on each other: start with tile efficiency (Tip 5) and the 5-block theory (Tip 1), then layer on yaku planning (Tip 2) and dora reading (Tip 6). Once you're comfortable with the offensive side, add defense (Tips 3, 8) and learn the push-fold decision (Tip 9). The riichi decision (Tip 4) and pinfu mastery (Tip 7) are the bridge between offense and defense — they're where you'll spend most of your development time as an intermediate player.

No single tip will transform your game overnight. But if you apply these principles consistently — one at a time, in focused practice sessions — you'll see steady improvement. The jump from "I know the rules" to "I know why I'm winning" is the most satisfying progression in riichi mahjong. And once you've made that jump, the game opens up in ways you can't imagine when you're still struggling to remember the yaku.
