RIICHI SCORING

Mahjong Scoring Guide: How Points Work in Riichi Mahjong

Riichi mahjong scoring looks intimidating, but it follows two simple ideas: han and fu. This guide breaks down the formula, the mangan cutoff, the scoring table, and walks through real hands so you can finally read your score with confidence.

The first time I won a hand in riichi mahjong, I had no idea how many points I had earned. I knew I had a yaku. I knew the hand was worth something. But the table in front of me was a wall of numbers, and the player next to me just told me a number that meant nothing. Was 2,000 a lot? Was 12,000 a lot? Why did the same hand pay differently when the dealer won it? If that sounds familiar, this guide is for you. Riichi mahjong scoring has a reputation for complexity, but once you understand the two pillars of the system — han and fu — everything else falls into place.

Why Scoring Matters in Riichi Mahjong

In many card games and casual board games, winning a round is what matters, and the points are an afterthought. Riichi mahjong is different. Because scoring is so granular, the value of your hand shapes almost every decision you make. Should you push for a bigger yaku or take the safe win? Should you declare riichi or stay in dama? Should you fold to defend against a dealer, or push because the deal-in penalty is small? None of those questions can be answered without understanding points.

Scoring also determines the rhythm of an entire match. A player who wins many small hands can stay competitive with a player who lands one big hand, but only if they understand how to read the score. A 1,000-point win is not the same as a 4,000-point win, and a 4,000-point deal-in is not the same as a 12,000-point deal-in. If you want to make good decisions about strategy tips like push-or-fold, you need to be able to estimate scores on sight.

Finally, scoring is the bridge between playing and improving. When you start seeing why one hand pays 2,000 and another pays 8,000, you start making different building choices. You stop chasing shapes that look flashy but score poorly. You start recognizing when a hand has mangan potential and when it does not. If you are new to the game, read our how-to-play guide first to learn the rules, then come back here.

The Two Scoring Factors: Han and Fu

Every winning hand in riichi mahjong is scored using two factors: han and fu. Han measures the difficulty and value of the hand. Fu measures the structural complexity of the hand. The final score is calculated by combining these two numbers through a formula, then adjusting for whether the winner is the dealer and whether they won by tsumo or ron.

Han: How Difficult the Hand Is

Han comes from two sources: yaku and dora. Each yaku is worth a fixed number of han, usually between 1 and 6. A simple yaku like riichi is worth 1 han. A harder yaku like toitoi (all triplets) is worth 2 han. A rare yaku like chinitsu (full flush, all tiles in one numbered suit) is worth 6 han when closed. You can see the full breakdown in our complete yaku list with han values.

Multiple yaku stack. A single winning hand might combine riichi (1 han), pinfu (1 han), tsumo (1 han), and one dora (1 han) for a total of 4 han. Stacking yaku is one of the main ways hands grow in value, and learning which yaku combine well is a core intermediate skill.

Dora tiles add han too. Every dora tile in your winning hand adds 1 han. If you declared riichi, you also gain access to ura-dora — dora hidden under the dora indicator, revealed only after a riichi win. A hand with 3 han from yaku and 4 han from dora is a 7-han hand, well into haneman territory. Dora can transform an otherwise modest hand into a major threat.

The most important han threshold to remember is 5 han = mangan. At 5 han, your hand is worth a fixed 8,000 points as a non-dealer (12,000 as a dealer), and fu no longer matters. This is the mangan cutoff, and it changes how you think about hand building. Once you are at 4 han, adding one more han is much more valuable than adding fu, because it doubles your score.

Fu: The Base Points of the Hand

If han is the headline, fu is the fine print. Fu measures how structurally complex your hand is, and it only matters below mangan. Once you hit mangan, fu is ignored. But for hands in the 1–4 han range, fu can be the difference between 1,000 points and 4,000 points.

Fu always starts at a base of 20 fu. From there, you add fu for several things:

  • Winning method: +10 fu if you won by ron (closed or open), +2 fu if you won by tsumo (unless your only yaku is pinfu, in which case tsumo adds 0 fu).
  • Melds: Open sequences add 0 fu. Open triplets of simple tiles (2–8) add 4 fu, open triplets of terminals/honors add 8 fu. Closed triplets (ankou) are worth double: 8 fu for simples, 16 fu for terminals and honors. Closed quads are worth 32 fu, open quads 16 fu.
  • The pair: If your pair is a dragon tile, your seat wind, or the round wind, you add 2 fu. If it is a double wind (both your seat wind and the round wind), it is still 2 fu, not 4.
  • The wait: If your winning tile completed a kanchan, penchan, or tanki wait, you add 2 fu. If it completed a ryanmen or shanpon wait, you add 0 fu.

After adding up all the fu, you round up to the nearest 10. The only exception is pinfu won by tsumo, which stays at exactly 20 fu and never rounds, because pinfu adds no fu from melds, pair, or wait.

A simple closed hand won by ron with no valuable melds sits at 30 fu (20 base + 10 for ron). A hand loaded with closed triplets of dragon tiles can easily climb to 60 or 70 fu. Fu rewards hands that are structurally dense and hard to build. The trade-off is that high-fu hands tend to use fewer sequences, which usually means fewer yaku and slower hand building.

The Scoring Formula Explained

Once you have your han and fu, you plug them into the basic points formula:

FORMULA

Basic Points

The basic points of a riichi mahjong hand are calculated as:

basic points = fu × 2^(han + 2)

This number is then capped at 2,000 (the mangan limit). Above mangan, the formula is replaced by fixed values for haneman, baiman, sanbaiman, and yakuman.

Why it matters: The formula grows exponentially in han but only linearly in fu. Doubling your han roughly quadruples your score, while doubling your fu only doubles it. That is why han is the dominant factor — one extra han almost always beats a lot of extra fu.

Let us plug in a concrete number. Say you have a hand worth 2 han and 30 fu. The basic points are 30 × 2^(2+2) = 30 × 16 = 480. From there, the actual payment is calculated based on whether you are the dealer and how you won. We will cover that next.

The key takeaway is that han growth is explosive, fu growth is steady. Going from 2 han to 3 han multiplies your basic points by 2. Going from 30 fu to 40 fu only adds about a third. Once you internalize this, you start to understand why players obsess over adding yaku and dora.

The Scoring Table: Mangan and Above

Once a hand reaches 5 han, the formula gets replaced by a fixed scoring ladder. This is the table every riichi player memorizes:

Hand Value Han Range Non-Dealer (Ron) Dealer (Ron)
Mangan 5 han, or 4 han / 30+ fu, or 3 han / 60+ fu 8,000 12,000
Haneman 6–7 han 12,000 18,000
Baiman 8–10 han 16,000 24,000
Sanbaiman 11–12 han 24,000 36,000
Yakuman 13+ han, or any named yakuman 32,000 48,000

Notice how each step is 1.5× the previous one. Mangan to haneman to baiman to sanbaiman to yakuman climbs in steady 1.5× jumps. That makes the ladder easy to remember: each tier is half-again the previous tier. Also notice that dealer values are exactly 1.5× the non-dealer values, all the way up.

How to Read a Scoring Table: Dealer vs Non-Dealer

A full riichi scoring table has four quadrants because there are two variables that change the payout: whether the winner is the dealer, and whether the win was by tsumo or ron. The dealer pays and receives 50% more than a non-dealer. So whenever you look up a score, the first question is always: am I the dealer?

When the dealer wins by ron, the discarding player pays the full amount. When the dealer wins by tsumo, each of the three non-dealer players pays an equal share. When a non-dealer wins by ron, the discarding player pays the full amount. When a non-dealer wins by tsumo, the dealer pays a larger share than the other two players.

Tsumo vs Ron: How the Payment Splits

The split rules are simple once you remember them:

  • Dealer tsumo: Each of the three non-dealer players pays the same amount, set at one-third of the dealer ron value.
  • Non-dealer tsumo: The dealer pays roughly half the total, and the other two non-dealers split the rest equally.
  • Ron (dealer or not): The discarding player pays the entire amount, no splitting.

This is why tsumo can feel surprisingly painful when you are not the dealer — everyone at the table pays, but the dealer pays extra. It is also why defending against a dealer in tenpai matters more than defending against a non-dealer: the deal-in penalty is 50% larger.

Worked Example 1: A Simple Pinfu Hand

Let us score a real hand. Imagine you are a non-dealer, you declared riichi, and you won by ron with a fully closed hand made of four sequences and one pair, with no valuable pair and a ryanmen wait. That is the classic pinfu shape.

EXAMPLE 1

Pinfu + Riichi, Non-Dealer Ron

Hand: Closed, four sequences + pair, ryanmen wait, won by ron.

Han: Pinfu (1) + Riichi (1) = 2 han.

Fu: Pinfu won by ron is always exactly 30 fu (20 base + 10 closed ron, no other additions because pinfu forbids them).

basic points = 30 × 2^(2+2) = 30 × 16 = 480
non-dealer ron = 4 × 480 = 1,920 → rounds up to 2,000
Result: You win 2,000 points from the player who discarded the winning tile. A small, clean win — this is the bread and butter of riichi play.

Notice that the rounding step is important. The basic points are 480, but the actual payment (4 × 480 = 1,920) rounds up to the nearest 100, giving you 2,000. This is why most riichi players end up memorizing the rounded values rather than calculating from scratch.

Worked Example 2: A Triplet-Heavy Hand

Now let us score a heavier hand. You are the dealer, you declared riichi, you won by tsumo, and your hand contains a closed triplet of green dragons (hatsu) plus a closed triplet of 5 of characters. Your wait is a tanki wait on the pair.

EXAMPLE 2

Rich Hand, Dealer Tsumo

Hand: Closed, contains ankou of hatsu (dragon) and ankou of 5 of characters, won by tsumo after riichi, tanki wait.

Han: Riichi (1) + Menzen Tsumo (1) + Hatsu yaku-pai (1) = 3 han (plus any dora in the hand, but let us assume zero for clarity).

Fu:

  • Base: 20
  • Tsumo: +2
  • Closed triplet of dragon (hatsu): +8 (ankou of yakuhai)
  • Closed triplet of 5 of characters (simple): +8 (ankou of simples)
  • Tanki wait: +2
  • Pair: 0 (let us assume a valueless pair)
  • Subtotal: 20 + 2 + 8 + 8 + 2 = 40 → rounds up to 40 fu
basic points = 40 × 2^(3+2) = 40 × 32 = 1,280
dealer tsumo = each non-dealer pays 2 × 1,280 = 2,560 → rounds to 2,600
total = 2,600 × 3 = 7,800
Result: Each non-dealer pays 2,600, and you collect a total of 7,800 points. A single dora would have pushed you into mangan territory for 12,000 total, which is why players chase dora so aggressively.

This example shows why fu matters for hands in the 1–4 han range. The two closed triplets added 16 fu, which more than doubled the score compared to a 20-fu version of the same hand. But compare that to adding a single dora, which would have pushed you into mangan for a 4,000-point swing. Han still wins.

Yakuman Hands: The Top of the Mountain

A yakuman is the highest single-tier hand in riichi mahjong. There are two ways to reach one. The first is to assemble a named yakuman hand: kokushi-musou (thirteen orphans), suuankou (four closed triplets), daisangen (big three dragons), tsuuiisou (all honors), suushi (four little winds), chinroutou (all terminals), ryuuiisou (all green), or chuuren poutou (nine gates). Each of these is worth a fixed 32,000 points (non-dealer) or 48,000 (dealer), regardless of fu.

The second way is kazoe-yakuman, which means "counted yakuman." If your hand reaches 13 or more han without containing a named yakuman, it is counted as a yakuman for scoring purposes. This usually happens when you stack dora on top of a strong yaku combination. A closed chinitsu with several dora and riichi/ura-dora can reach kazoe-yakuman territory.

Multiple yakuman can stack. Some hands combine two or even more yakuman. For example, a closed hand with four closed triplets of honors and a pair of honors is both suuankou and tsuuiisou, which counts as a double yakuman worth 64,000 points (non-dealer) or 96,000 (dealer). The biggest legal single hand in standard rules is a triple yakuman, worth 96,000 or 144,000 points. These hands are extraordinarily rare, but they are why scoring tables extend so high.

When to Push for Higher Han vs Higher Fu

One of the most common decisions in riichi mahjong is whether to chase more han or to settle for more fu. The right answer depends on the situation, but a few principles will guide you well:

  • Below 4 han, chase han first. Because the formula is exponential in han, one extra han almost always beats one extra fu. If your hand has any realistic path to another yaku or to a dora, take it.
  • At 4 han with low fu, push for mangan. Going from 4 han / 30 fu to 5 han jumps your score from roughly 7,700 to 8,000 (mangan). The marginal effort is small, but the jump to haneman (12,000) is much bigger, so once you are at mangan, do not bother chasing more fu.
  • At 3 han with very high fu, you may already be near mangan. A 3-han hand with 60 fu is mangan. Hands like toitoi with closed terminal triplets can hit this. In these cases, adding one more han for haneman is a clear jump, but adding more fu is wasted.
  • When in doubt, declare riichi. Riichi gives you 1 han, access to ura-dora, and forces opponents to defend. For a closed hand that is already in tenpai, riichi is almost always the right call — it converts a low-value hand into a mangan-tier threat when the ura-dora align.
  • Avoid chasing fu at the cost of speed. A 70-fu hand that takes 12 turns to build is usually worse than a 30-fu hand that wins in 6 turns. Speed compounds; fu does not.

Common Scoring Pitfalls Beginners Make

Scoring is full of small traps. Here are the mistakes we see most often, and they are the same ones covered in our guide to scoring mistakes for beginners:

  • Forgetting the +10 fu for closed ron. A closed hand won by ron is 30 fu, not 20. This is the most common fu mistake, and it changes your score by a meaningful margin on every closed ron win.
  • Confusing pinfu tsumo with pinfu ron. Pinfu by tsumo is 20 fu flat. Pinfu by ron is 30 fu. Mixing these up is easy, and it leads to wrong scores in casual games.
  • Not knowing about the mangan cap. A 4-han hand with 110 fu is still only mangan. Fu cannot push you past mangan, no matter how high it goes. Many beginners overvalue high-fu hands because they assume more fu always means more points.
  • Treating dora as a yaku. Dora tiles add han, but they are not a yaku. You still need at least one yaku to win. A hand full of dora but with no yaku cannot win at all — you would be stuck in furiten-like situations, unable to claim a win.
  • Forgetting dealer vs non-dealer. The same hand pays 50% more when the dealer wins it. Players often estimate the value of a deal-in based on their own seat, then get surprised when the dealer is the one who wins.
  • Ignoring the round-up to nearest 100. The final payment is always rounded up to the nearest 100. Small hands often jump a few hundred points because of rounding, which matters in close games.
  • Counting seat winds as double. A pair that is both your seat wind and the round wind is worth 2 fu, not 4 fu. The bonus does not stack.

Once you stop making these mistakes, your score estimates will be close enough to use at the table. You do not need to be exact in your head — you need to know whether a deal-in costs 2,000 or 8,000. That single distinction changes your entire strategy.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is mahjong scored in riichi mahjong?

Riichi mahjong scoring combines two factors: han (difficulty, from yaku and dora) and fu (base points from melds, waits, and winning method). The basic points are calculated as fu multiplied by 2 to the power of (han + 2), then rounded and capped at mangan. The final payment depends on whether the winner is the dealer or not, and whether they won by tsumo or ron.

What is mangan in riichi mahjong?

Mangan is the first major scoring ceiling in riichi mahjong. It is worth 8,000 points for a non-dealer win by ron, or 12,000 points for a dealer win by ron. A hand reaches mangan at exactly 5 han, or at 4 han with at least 30 fu, or at 3 han with at least 60 fu. Once a hand hits mangan, fu no longer matters, and the score jumps in fixed steps to haneman, baiman, sanbaiman, and yakuman.

How many han do you get for riichi?

Calling riichi gives you 1 han. If you win after declaring riichi, you also gain access to ura-dora, which can add another 1 to 5+ han on top. Riichi is one of the easiest ways to add value to an otherwise low-scoring hand, which is why experienced players declare it whenever they have a fast, safe wait.

What is the difference between han and fu?

Han measures how difficult or valuable your hand is, earned from yaku and dora tiles. Fu measures the structural complexity of the hand, earned from triplets, quads, pair tiles, wait types, and how you won. Han grows in big jumps and dominates the score, while fu only matters below the mangan cutoff. A hand with high han and low fu can easily outscore a hand with low han and high fu.

What is a yakuman hand worth?

A single yakuman is worth 32,000 points for a non-dealer ron or 48,000 points for a dealer ron. Multiple yakuman can stack, so a double yakuman is worth 64,000 non-dealer or 96,000 dealer. Hands worth 13 or more han without a named yakuman are counted as kazoe-yakuman and scored at the same 32,000 base value.

Final Thoughts

Riichi mahjong scoring is one of those topics that seems impenetrable from the outside and obvious from the inside. The formula, the rounding, the dealer multipliers — they look like a wall of rules. But once you have scored a dozen real hands, the system becomes second nature. You stop calculating and start seeing. A 2-han pinfu hand is 2,000. A mangan-tier threat is 8,000. A yakuman is 32,000. Those three numbers cover most of what you will encounter at the table.

The biggest mental shift is learning to chase han, not fu. Fu is fine print. Han is the headline. Whenever you have a choice between adding complexity to your hand (more fu) and adding difficulty (more han), choose han — unless you are already at mangan, in which case fu becomes irrelevant anyway. That single principle, applied consistently, will move your average score from low to mangan-tier and beyond.

Finally, do not try to memorize the full scoring table on day one. Start by learning the mangan cutoff and the four tiers above it.
