Mahjong Yaku List

Every major riichi mahjong yaku in one place. Han values, English names, romaji, and quick descriptions. Deep-link any yaku with its anchor.

This is the complete riichi mahjong yaku list — every winning hand pattern recognized in modern Japanese mahjong, with the English name, romaji, kanji, and han value for each. Whether you're learning the game for the first time or refreshing your memory between rounds, you can use this as your reference.

New to the game? Start with the 1-han yaku at the top (Riichi, Tanyao, Pinfu, Yakuhai) — they're the patterns you'll see in 80% of winning hands. Work your way down as you get comfortable. For the full rules behind how these patterns form, see our beginner's guide to riichi mahjong.

Yaku FAQ

What is a yaku in riichi mahjong?

A yaku is a specific winning hand pattern in Japanese riichi mahjong. To win, your hand must contain at least one yaku, which determines your base han value. Examples include Riichi (declaring readiness), Tanyao (all simples 2-8), and Pinfu (all-sequences with no fu points). Without a yaku, even a complete 14-tile hand cannot win.

How many yaku are in riichi mahjong?

Standard riichi mahjong has roughly 40 recognized yaku patterns, plus a handful of yakuman (limit hands) worth the maximum score instantly. Our list above covers the 16 most common yaku that account for the vast majority of winning hands in real play. The rarer patterns can be learned later once these are second nature.

What is the easiest yaku to get?

The easiest yaku for beginners are Riichi (just declare when one tile from winning), Tanyao (keep all tiles in the 2-8 range), and Yakuhai (a triplet of any dragon tile or your seat wind). Pinfu is also accessible once you learn to build all-sequence hands with a non-pair head. These four yaku will carry you through your first 50+ games.

Can you stack multiple yaku in one hand?

Yes — and you should. Most winning hands combine 2-4 yaku, with each yaku adding its han value to the total. For example, Riichi + Tanyao + Pinfu = 3 han. Higher han totals mean exponentially higher scores: a 4-han hand is worth about 5x a 1-han hand, and a yakuman (typically 13+ han) is worth the maximum score instantly.

What is the difference between han and fu?

Han is the multiplier from your yaku patterns — each yaku is worth 1, 2, 3, or 6 han (or yakuman level). Fu is a base point value calculated from how "valuable" your melds are (triplets vs. sequences, closed vs. open, etc.). Final score = base fu × 2^(han+2), capped at various mangan limits. For beginners, focus on collecting han — fu optimization comes later.

What is a yakuman?

A yakuman is a rare, exceptionally valuable hand pattern worth the maximum score instantly (typically 32,000 points for a non-dealer, 48,000 for the dealer). Examples include Kokushi Musou (13-orphans — one of every terminal and honor tile plus a duplicate), Chuuren Poutou (9 gates pattern), and Suuankou (four concealed triplets). They're rare enough that many players go years without seeing one in a real game.

Do I need to memorize all these yaku to play?

No. You can play competently knowing just Riichi, Tanyao, Pinfu, and Yakuhai — that's enough to win most rounds. The rest come naturally as you play. We recommend bookmarking this yaku list and referring to it between rounds. In our browser game, the score breakdown panel shows which yaku your hand scored, so you learn by doing.
