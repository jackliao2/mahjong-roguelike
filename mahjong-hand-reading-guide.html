ANALYSIS SKILLS

Mahjong Hand Reading: A Step-by-Step Guide

Hand reading is the skill that turns a pile of tiles into a plan. Learn to analyze any hand in five clear steps.

When I first looked at a mahjong hand, I saw 13 separate tiles. After learning hand reading, I saw a pair, two completed sequences, a partial sequence, and a floating tile. Same hand, completely different understanding. Hand reading is the lens that makes everything else in mahjong make sense. This guide will teach you to put that lens on.

What Is Hand Reading?

Hand reading is the process of analyzing a mahjong hand to understand what it is, what it needs, and where it is going. It covers your own hand and, to some degree, your opponents' hands. Good hand reading lets you choose better discards, pick the right yaku, know when you are close to winning, and defend against players who are.

This guide breaks hand reading into five steps. Practice them in order until they become automatic. Then start applying them to both offense and defense.

Step 1: Count the Blocks

Every winning hand has five blocks: four melds plus one pair. When you look at your hand, the first thing to do is group the tiles into candidate blocks. Completed sequences and triplets are full blocks. Pairs are blocks. Two-tile sequences are partial blocks that want to become full blocks.

Counting blocks tells you the shape of your hand. If you have six blocks, you have redundancy. You can discard from the weakest block. If you have four blocks, your hand is slow and you need to develop more shapes. If you have five, your hand is balanced and you should focus on efficiency.

This step is the foundation of the 5-block theory. Do not move to step 2 until you can count blocks quickly and accurately.

Step 2: Identify Possible Yaku

Once you know the shape, ask what yaku the hand can support. Look at the tile composition. Are most tiles numbered 2 through 8? Tanyao is possible. Are there four sequences and no honor tiles? Pinfu is possible. Is there a pair of dragons with a third one nearby? Yakuhai is possible.

You do not need to commit to a yaku immediately, but you should know the options. A hand with no viable yaku path is a problem. If you cannot identify at least one reasonable yaku by turn four, consider shifting toward defense or a simple all-simples plan.

For a complete reference of yaku, see our yaku list. Knowing the yaku is what turns block counting into real strategy.

Step 3: Calculate Shanten

Shanten tells you how many tiles away your hand is from tenpai. A tenpai hand has 0 shanten. A hand that needs one useful tile to reach tenpai has 1 shanten. A hand that needs two useful tiles has 2 shanten.

Calculating shanten is easier than it sounds. After counting blocks, look at how many of your blocks are incomplete. Each incomplete block usually needs one or two tiles. The total distance is your shanten. With practice, you will estimate shanten in seconds.

Shanten is important because it tells you how aggressive to be. A 1-shanten hand can push. A 3-shanten hand should play carefully, especially if opponents are showing speed. Do not commit heavily to a hand that is far from completion.

Step 4: Find the Best Discard

Now that you know your blocks, yaku, and shanten, choose a discard. The best discard is usually the one that improves your hand the most while keeping it safe. This is where ukeire comes in. Count how many tiles would improve your hand for each possible discard, and pick the one with the highest number.

But ukeire is not the only factor. You also need to consider:

  • Yaku fit: Does the discard preserve your intended yaku?
  • Safety: Is the discard dangerous given your opponents' discards?
  • Wait quality: Will the resulting tenpai have a good wait?
  • Dora value: Are you throwing away a valuable tile?

The best discard balances all four. Early in the hand, lean toward ukeire and yaku fit. Late in the hand, lean toward safety.

Step 5: Read Your Opponents

Hand reading is not just about your own hand. You can also read opponents by watching their discards and calls. This is harder and less certain, but it is crucial for defense.

Here are the main signals:

  • Suit concentration: A player discarding mostly one suit is likely building a half-flush or full-flush hand.
  • Honor discards: Early honor discards often mean a fast, simple hand. Late honor discards can mean the player is close to tenpai and clearing safe tiles.
  • Open calls: A pon on dragons or a wind tile suggests yakuhai value. A chii early suggests a sequence-based hand.
  • Riichi declaration: The strongest signal of all. Treat it as a warning that the player is one tile from winning and adjust your discards accordingly.

Opponent reading is probabilistic, not certain. You are trying to reduce risk, not predict exact hands. Combine these signals with genbutsu, suji, and kabe defense to stay safe.

HAND READING CHECKLIST

Five Questions Before Every Discard

Use this checklist until it becomes automatic. How many blocks do I have? What yaku am I aiming for? What is my shanten? Which discard has the highest ukeire? Is the discard safe enough for the current board?

Why it works: These five questions cover offense, defense, and planning. Running through them prevents autopilot mistakes and improves every decision.

Applying Hand Reading to Defense

The same five steps work in reverse when you defend. Look at an opponent's discards and try to reconstruct their hand. What blocks could they have? What yaku could they be aiming for? What tiles would complete their hand?

If an opponent declares riichi, ask what waits are likely. Have they discarded mostly low tiles? They might be waiting on a high tile. Have they discarded from one suit heavily? They might be waiting in another suit. Use these guesses to avoid discarding their likely winning tiles.

Common Hand Reading Mistakes

Even experienced players make these errors. Watch out for them.

  • Forcing a yaku: Do not commit to half-flush or seven pairs unless the starting hand supports it.
  • Ignoring dora: The dora tile changes the value of your hand. Read it into your plan.
  • Counting shapes twice: A 2-3-4-5 shape can be grouped as 2-3-4 and 3-4-5, but it is still one sequence and one partial, not two full sequences.
  • Over-reading opponents: You cannot know their exact hand. Use probabilities, not assumptions.

Practice Drills

Hand reading is a skill you build through repetition. Try these drills.

  • Block count drill: Deal 13 tiles and count blocks in under 5 seconds.
  • Yaku quiz: Look at a hand and name every yaku it could aim for.
  • Shanten drill: Estimate shanten for 10 hands in a row, then verify.
  • Mahjong Quiz: Use our hand-reading questions to drill real positions with instant feedback.

The goal is to make the five steps automatic. When you can read a hand in seconds, you will have more mental energy left for defense and advanced decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is hand reading in mahjong?

Hand reading is the process of analyzing a mahjong hand to understand what it needs, what yaku it can achieve, and how close it is to winning. It includes counting blocks, calculating shanten, identifying waits, and reading opponents' discards.

How do I count blocks in a mahjong hand?

Group your 13 tiles into candidate melds and one pair. Every completed sequence or triplet is one block. Every pair is one block. Every two-tile partial sequence is a partial block. A winning hand needs exactly five blocks: four melds plus one pair.

What does shanten mean?

Shanten is the number of tiles your hand is away from tenpai. A tenpai hand has 0 shanten. A hand that needs one more useful tile has 1 shanten. Lower shanten means your hand is closer to completion.

Can I read my opponents' hands too?

Yes, though not perfectly. You can estimate what opponents are building by watching their discards, calls, and honor tile priorities. If a player discards mostly circles, they may be building a characters or bamboo hand. If they call pon on dragons early, they are likely aiming for yakuhai value.

What is the fastest way to improve hand reading?

The fastest way is deliberate repetition. Use Mahjong Quiz to drill hand-reading questions, practice counting blocks on real starting hands, and review hands after each game. Pattern recognition develops quickly with focused practice.

Final Thoughts

Hand reading is the central skill of riichi mahjong. It connects rules to strategy, defense to offense, and luck to decision-making. The five steps in this guide are simple, but mastering them takes time.

Start by counting blocks on every hand. Add yaku identification next. Then shanten, then discard selection, then opponent reads. Build the skill layer by layer, and soon you will look at any mahjong hand and see a clear plan instead of a random pile of tiles.
