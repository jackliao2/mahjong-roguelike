TILE REFERENCE

Mahjong Tile Meanings: Complete Visual Guide to All 136 Tiles

Every tile in a riichi mahjong set tells a story. From the coins of the Pin suit to the dragons of the honors, this guide breaks down all 136 tiles so you can recognize them instantly.

The first time I opened a real mahjong set, I dumped all 136 tiles onto the table and felt my stomach drop. There were tiles covered in Chinese characters, tiles with stacks of coins, tiles with bamboo sticks, and a handful of colored dragons I could not tell apart. I had played card games my whole life, but a deck of cards has 52 pieces. Mahjong has nearly three times that, and at first glance almost none of them looked alike. What I did not know then is that mahjong tiles are deeply organized. Once you understand the structure, the 136 tiles collapse into just 34 types, and those 34 types fall into a handful of clean groups that anyone can learn in an afternoon.

This guide walks through every tile in a standard riichi mahjong set. You will learn the three numbered suits (Man, Pin, and Sou), the honor tiles (winds and dragons), the notation players use to write tiles online, the difference between terminal and simple tiles, and the cultural history behind the designs. By the end, you will look at any tile and know its name, its suit, and its role in the game. If you are completely new, pair this guide with our how-to-play walkthrough to see how the tiles come together into winning hands.

How Many Tiles Are in a Mahjong Set?

A standard riichi mahjong set contains 136 tiles. That sounds like a lot, but the number comes from a simple formula: 34 distinct tile types, each with four identical copies. When you understand the 34 types, you understand the whole set. The four copies of each type exist so that players can form pairs, triplets, and quads from the wall during play. Without that symmetry, the math of waiting tiles and tile efficiency would not work.

The 34 tile types split into two big families. The first family is the numbered suits, also called the simple suits. There are three of these suits — Man, Pin, and Sou — and each one runs from 1 to 9, with four copies of each number. That gives us 9 × 3 × 4 = 108 numbered tiles. The second family is the honor tiles, which are not numbered. There are 7 distinct honor types (4 winds and 3 dragons), again with four copies each, for 28 honor tiles. Add them together and you get 108 + 28 = 136.

Riichi mahjong uses the 136-tile set described above. Other variants of mahjong, particularly Chinese mahjong and American mahjong, add extra tiles such as the four flowers and four seasons, bringing the total to 144 or more. Those bonus tiles do not appear in riichi, so this guide focuses exclusively on the 136 riichi tiles. If you ever play another variant, the suits and honors covered here will still apply — you will just see a few bonus tiles on top.

The Three Numbered Suits

The numbered suits are the heart of every mahjong hand. Each suit runs from 1 to 9, and sequences are built from three consecutive numbers in the same suit. A 4-5-6 of Pin is a valid sequence. A 4 of Pin, 5 of Sou, and 6 of Man is not, because the three tiles belong to different suits. Learning to keep suits separate in your head is one of the first skills a new player needs.

Man (萬): The Character Suit

The Man suit, sometimes called the character or crak suit, is the easiest to recognize by its top-heavy design. Every Man tile shows the Chinese numeral for its rank (一, 二, 三, 四, 五, 六, 七, 八, 九) written in a bold vertical brushstroke at the top, with the large red character 萬 underneath. The character 萬 means "ten thousand," which is why this suit is sometimes called the "myriad" suit. The 1 of Man (一萬) is the most iconic tile in the set: a single horizontal stroke above the red 萬 character. Many players use the 1m as their mental anchor for the whole suit.

The Man suit is the most text-heavy of the three. If you cannot yet read Chinese numerals, the shapes of the strokes themselves become a reliable cue. The number 1 (一) is a single horizontal line. The number 2 (二) is two horizontal lines. The number 3 (三) is three horizontal lines. Numbers 4 through 9 have more complex forms, but with a few repetitions you will start to recognize them at a glance. The red 萬 below the numeral is the constant that tells you which suit you are looking at.

Pin (筒): The Circle Suit

The Pin suit, also called the circle or dot suit, is the friendliest suit for beginners because the design is purely visual. Each Pin tile shows a pattern of round coins, and the number of coins on the tile equals the tile's rank. The 1 of Pin (1p) is a single large circle, often painted with a decorative pattern. The 2 of Pin (2p) shows two stacked coins. The 3 of Pin (3p) shows three coins arranged diagonally, and so on up to 9 of Pin (9p), which shows nine coins in a three-by-three grid.

The Pin suit is thought to originate from representations of Chinese copper coins strung together, the same coins that inspired the Man character 萬. Because the Pin designs are visual rather than textual, this is usually the first suit new players learn to read with confidence. If you are just starting out, train yourself on Pin first. It builds the habit of looking at tiles as visual patterns instead of as text to decode.

Sou (索): The Bamboo Suit

The Sou suit, also called the bamboo or stick suit, shows vertical bamboo sticks on each tile, with one stick for the 1 and the matching number of sticks for tiles 2 through 9. The 1 of Sou (1s) is special. Instead of a single bamboo stick, it shows a bird, usually a sparrow or a peacock. This bird tile is one of the most recognizable in the whole set, and it has deep cultural roots: the word for mahjong (麻雀) literally means "sparrow" in several dialects, and the bird on the 1s is a nod to that origin.

Sou tiles 2 through 9 are drawn as bundles of green bamboo sticks, usually with a red accent at the top of each stick to suggest a leaf or bud. The number of sticks matches the rank, so a 4 of Sou (4s) has four sticks, a 7 of Sou (7s) has seven sticks, and so on. The 1s bird and the consistent bamboo design make the Sou suit easy to distinguish from Man and Pin once you have seen the set a few times.

How to Read Tile Notation

Players do not write out "the 5 of circles" every time they discuss a hand. Instead, riichi mahjong uses a compact notation that pairs a number with a single letter for the suit. Once you learn it, you can describe any hand in a single line.

Notation Suit Example meaning
1m, 2m, 3m ... 9m Man (characters) 1m = 1 of characters, 9m = 9 of characters
1p, 2p, 3p ... 9p Pin (circles) 5p = 5 of circles, 7p = 7 of circles
1s, 2s, 3s ... 9s Sou (bamboo) 1s = 1 of bamboo (the bird), 7s = 7 of bamboo
E, S, W, N Winds E = East, S = South, W = West, N = North
d or 5z / 6z / 7z Dragons 5z = White, 6z = Green, 7z = Red (notation varies)

The numbered notation is intuitive: the number is the rank, and the letter is the suit's first letter. So 1m means 1 of Man, 5p means 5 of Pin, and 7s means 7 of Sou. Honor tiles use letters directly. Winds are simply E, S, W, N. Dragons are a little less standardized — some communities write them as H (haku, white), G (hatsu, green), and R (chun, red), while others use a numbered honor notation where 5z is white, 6z is green, and 7z is red. If you read strategy articles online, you will see both. When in doubt, the context usually makes it clear.

Notation also lets you describe full hands compactly. A hand like "123m 456p 789s EE 99p" tells another player exactly what tiles you hold: a sequence of 1-2-3 of Man, a sequence of 4-5-6 of Pin, a sequence of 7-8-9 of Sou, a pair of East winds, and a pair of 9 of Pin. Once you internalize the notation, reading and discussing hands becomes far faster.

Read Tiles in Suit Blocks

When you describe a hand, always group tiles by suit before reading them out. Instead of "5p, 2m, 9s, 3m," say "2m 3m, 5p, 9s." This habit makes patterns jump out: pairs, sequences, and shapes become obvious, and other players will follow your thinking faster.

Why it matters: Tile efficiency is all about spotting shapes. Grouping by suit forces you to see the structure of your hand, not just a list of tiles. This is the same habit strong players use to read tenpai hands in seconds.

The Honor Tiles: Winds and Dragons

Honor tiles sit outside the numbered suits. They cannot form sequences — there is no "sequence" of East, South, West the way there is a sequence of 4-5-6 of Pin. Instead, honors form pairs and triplets. They are also the foundation of many high-value yaku, because honors tend to be harder to collect and more rewarding when you do.

The Four Winds

The wind tiles are East (東), South (南), West (西), and North (北). Each is shown as a single large Chinese character on a plain background. The four winds appear once each in the set, with four copies per wind, for 16 wind tiles total. The characters themselves are among the most recognizable in mahjong: 東 is a complex shape often associated with the rising sun, while 西 and 北 are simpler. South (南) is the wind that beginners most often confuse with other characters, but with a little practice it stands out clearly.

Winds have a special role in scoring. The round wind (the prevailing wind of the current hand) and your seat wind (the position you are sitting in) both score as yaku when you complete a triplet of that wind. In an East-round game, a triplet of East (東) is worth a yaku. If you are seated South, a triplet of South (南) is also worth a yaku. This dual scoring rule is one reason winds matter so much: a single triplet can quietly lock in a yaku that lifts your whole hand's value.

The Three Dragons

The dragon tiles are the most colorful honors in the set. There are exactly three dragons, each with four copies, for 12 dragon tiles total.

  • White Dragon (白, haku): Usually shown as a plain white tile, sometimes with a thin blue border or a single thin rectangle. It is the only tile in the set that is essentially blank, which makes it instantly recognizable. Players new to the game often wonder whether they have a misprinted tile the first time they see a white dragon.
  • Green Dragon (發, hatsu): A large green character 發, meaning "to prosper" or "to issue." This character is heavily associated with wealth and good fortune in Chinese culture, which is why it appears in many shop fronts and decorations outside mahjong. On the tile, the green ink stands out sharply against the cream background.
  • Red Dragon (中, chun): A bold red character 中, meaning "center." This is the same character that appears on the back of many mahjong sets and on the logo of the Mahjong Quiz site itself. The red dragon is one of the most iconic tiles in the game.

Every dragon triplet is worth a yaku on its own, regardless of round or seat. That makes dragons more universally valuable than winds: a triplet of any dragon always scores, while a triplet of a wind only scores when it matches the round or your seat. This is why strong players treat dragon pairs and triplets as anchors around which to build a hand.

Terminal Tiles vs Simple Tiles

Within the numbered suits, tiles fall into two groups that behave very differently. The terminal tiles are the 1s and 9s of each suit — 1m, 9m, 1p, 9p, 1s, and 9s, six tile types in total. The simple tiles are the 2s through 8s of each suit, eighteen tile types in total. The distinction matters for both strategy and scoring.

Strategically, simple tiles are easier to build sequences around because they connect in two directions. A 5 of Pin can be part of a 3-4-5, a 4-5-6, a 5-6-7, or — when waiting — a 4-5 ryanmen waiting on 3 or 6. A terminal like 1 of Pin only connects in one direction: it can start a 1-2-3 sequence, but there is no 0 to extend below it. This is why terminals are discarded earlier and more often than simples, and why hands built mostly from terminals are harder to complete.

For scoring, terminals and honors are grouped together as jihai-adjacent high-value tiles. Yaku like junchan (all sequences and triplets contain at least one terminal) and honroutou (all tiles are terminals or honors) reward hands that lean on terminals. The classic chinroutou yaku, one of the rarest in the game, is a hand made entirely of terminals. Why Each Tile Has Four Copies

The four-copy rule is the spine of mahjong's design. Without it, the entire structure of the game would collapse. Here is why four copies is the magic number.

First, four copies make quads possible. A quad (kan) is a complete set of all four copies of a single tile. Quads are rare and valuable, and they trigger special rules: a new replacement tile is drawn from the dead wall, and the quad often counts toward extra scoring. Without four copies, quads could not exist.

Second, four copies make wait-counting work. When you are in tenpai, you count how many of your waiting tile are still unseen to estimate your chance of winning. If you hold one copy of a tile, three remain unseen. If you hold two, two remain. This arithmetic only works because the total number of each tile is fixed and known. To see how this plays out at the table, our waiting tiles guide walks through the counting in real hand shapes.

Third, four copies give the game a balance between rarity and attainability. If each tile had only two copies, completing triplets would be nearly impossible. If each had eight, the game would be too easy and the wall would be enormous. Four is the sweet spot, and it has been the standard for mahjong sets worldwide for over a century.

The Cultural History of Tile Designs

Mahjong tile designs are not arbitrary. They carry centuries of Chinese visual culture, and understanding that history makes the tiles easier to remember because the imagery starts to make sense.

The Pin suit is widely believed to derive from strings of Chinese cash coins, the round copper coins with a square hole in the middle that were used as currency for over two thousand years. The coins on Pin tiles are stylized versions of those real coins, stacked in patterns that reflect how they were strung in bundles of one hundred or one thousand. The 1 of Pin, with its single large ornate circle, is thought to represent a single coin displayed face-on.

The Sou suit is linked to bamboo tally sticks, which were used in ancient China as records of debts and contracts. A bamboo tally would be split in half, with each party keeping one half; matching the halves verified the agreement. The vertical sticks on the Sou tiles echo those tallies, and the bird on the 1 of Sou references the game's alternate name (麻雀, "sparrows"), which itself is said to come from the clattering sound tiles make on the table, likened to sparrows chattering.

The Man suit is the most overtly textual. The character 萬 means "ten thousand," and historically the Man suit may have represented strings of ten thousand coins — the largest denomination of cash. The numerals on top of the 萬 character are written in formal Chinese script, the same forms used in accounting and calligraphy for centuries. This is why the Man suit looks the most like a piece of written language rather than a picture.

The dragons carry meaning too. The green 發 (hatsu) literally means "to prosper" or "to issue forth," and it appears in countless Chinese New Year decorations. The red 中 (chun) means "center" and is associated with the concept of the middle path and with the Chinese character for China itself (中国). The white dragon (白, haku) means "white" or "pure," and its plain face on the tile is itself the symbol — the absence of decoration is the decoration.

None of this history is required to play, but it makes the tiles feel less arbitrary. Once you know that Pin comes from coins and Sou from bamboo tallies, you stop seeing them as random shapes and start seeing them as a small museum of Chinese material culture.

Practical Tips for Recognizing Tiles Fast

Knowing what each tile means is one thing. Recognizing it instantly during a fast game is another. Here are the techniques that experienced players use to build speed.

  • Learn one suit to fluency first. Pick Pin (circles), because the designs are purely visual and the count of coins matches the rank. Drill yourself on Pin until you can name each tile in under a second, then add Sou, then Man. Trying to learn all three at once is overwhelming.
  • Anchor on the 1 of each suit. The 1m (single stroke + 萬), 1p (single large circle), and 1s (the bird) are the most distinctive tiles in their suits. Once you can identify the 1 of a suit, the rest of the suit becomes a count: "this looks like the 1 of Sou, but with seven sticks instead of a bird, so it must be 7s."
  • Memorize the dragon colors, not the characters. If you do not read Chinese, focus on the color: white is blank, green is 發, red is 中. The colors are consistent across virtually every mahjong set ever made.
  • Group winds by stroke count. East (東) is the busiest character, North (北) is the simplest. South (南) and West (西) sit in between. With a few repetitions you will sort them by visual complexity, which is faster than reading each character.
  • Drill with quiz mode. The fastest way to lock in tile recognition is to answer timed questions. Our Mahjong Quiz shows you tiles in real hand contexts and asks you to identify waits, sequences, and yaku. A few minutes a day builds permanent recognition.

Tile recognition becomes automatic with surprisingly little practice. Most new players reach fluent recognition within a week of regular play, and the skill is permanent — once your brain learns the shapes, you do not forget them. The only barrier is the first hour or two of confusion, which every player goes through. Push through it and the tiles will feel as familiar as playing cards.

Putting It All Together

A complete riichi mahjong set has 136 tiles, organized into 34 types with four copies each. The three numbered suits — Man (characters, 萬), Pin (circles, 筒), and Sou (bamboo, 索) — each run from 1 to 9 and supply 108 of the 136 tiles. The honor tiles — four winds (East, South, West, North) and three dragons (White, Green, Red) — supply the remaining 28. Terminal tiles (the 1s and 9s) behave differently from simple tiles (the 2s through 8s) and unlock specific high-value yaku. Notation like 1m, 5p, and 7s lets you describe any hand compactly, and the four-copy rule makes quads, wait-counting, and the entire strategic depth of the game possible.

Once you can name every tile, the rest of mahjong opens up. You can read about waiting tiles, study yaku patterns, or drill hand-reading without stopping to translate each tile you see. Tile recognition is the gateway skill, and you have just walked through it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many tiles are in a standard riichi mahjong set?

A standard riichi mahjong set contains 136 tiles: 34 distinct tile types with four copies of each. The 34 types break down into 27 numbered suit tiles (1 through 9 of Man, Pin, and Sou) and 7 honor tiles (four winds and three dragons).

What do the three mahjong suits mean?

The three suits are Man (characters, marked with the kanji 萬 and a number), Pin (circles, shown as stacked coins), and Sou (bamboo, drawn as sticks). Each suit has tiles numbered 1 through 9 with four copies of each, for 36 tiles per suit and 108 numbered tiles total.

How do I read mahjong tile notation like 5p or 7s?

Tile notation pairs the number with a suit letter: m for Man, p for Pin, and s for Sou. So 5p means the 5 of circles, 7s means the 7 of bamboo, and 1m means the 1 of characters. Honor tiles use letters: E, S, W, N for winds and d for dragons (often written as 5z for white, 6z for green, 7z for red).

Why are there four copies of each mahjong tile?

Four copies allow triplets, quads, and pairs to be drawn naturally from the wall during play. With four copies, a player holding one tile knows three more remain unseen, which makes wait-counting and tile efficiency math possible. This symmetry is what gives riichi mahjong its depth.

What is the difference between terminal and simple tiles?

Terminal tiles are the 1s and 9s of each suit. Simple tiles are the 2 through 8 of each suit. Terminals are harder to complete into sequences because they only connect in one direction, while simple tiles connect in two directions. Many yaku, like junchan and honroutou, reward hands built around terminals and honors.

Final Thoughts

The 136 tiles of a riichi mahjong set look like a wall of unfamiliar symbols the first time you see them. They are not. They are a small, well-organized collection of 34 types, each tied to a clear visual theme and a slice of cultural history. The Man suit tells you about Chinese numerals and currency. The Pin suit shows you the coins that inspired it. The Sou suit traces back to bamboo tallies and the sparrows that gave the game its name. The winds and dragons layer on top with characters that have been painted on storefronts and New Year decorations for generations.

Learn the suits one at a time. Anchor on the distinctive tiles — the 1 of each suit, the colored dragons, the wind characters. Drill the notation until 1m, 5p, and 7s feel as natural as reading a sentence. Then put it all into practice. The fastest way to make tile recognition permanent is to play, and the fastest way to play is to open the quiz and answer real questions. Once the tiles are familiar, every other part of mahjong — the yaku, the waits, the strategy — becomes something you can study instead of something you have to translate.
